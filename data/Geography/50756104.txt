[[File:Alcohol free zone Liverpool.jpg|thumb|upright=0.9|Signage for an alcohol-free zone in [[Liverpool]], England]]

An '''alcohol-free zone''' is a geographic area, location or establishment where the public consumption and sale of [[alcoholic beverage]]s is prohibited.<ref name="Barclay 2016"/><ref name="Saggers Gray 1998"/> Alcohol-free zones have been established in some areas to address problems with drinking- and binge drinking-related crime, antisocial behavior, assaults and disorderly behavior.<ref name="Mazerolle Ransley 2006"/><ref name="Martin Lancer 2015"/> Alcohol-free zones have been opposed in some communities, such as in [[Bath, Somerset|Bath]], England, which has "a tradition of open-air bars and restaurants."<ref name="Mazerolle Ransley 2006"/>

==Alcohol-free zones==
===Australia===
In [[New South Wales]], Australia, some streets are designated as alcohol-free zones, which is demarcated with signage stating such.<ref name="Sanders 2010"/> Police may issue a warning to violators, and those who continue to drink can be fined and have their alcoholic beverages seized.<ref name="Sanders 2010"/> Many parks in New South Wales are designated as alcohol-free zones.<ref name="Sanders 2010"/> [[Bondi, New South Wales|Bondi]] in New South Wales, Australia established an alcohol-free zone in the city in 2004 in efforts to reduce assaults and antisocial behavior influenced by street drinking.<ref name="Mazerolle Ransley 2006"/>

As of April 2016, [[Sydney]], Australia has over 60 alcohol-free zones.<ref name="Barclay 2016"/> The City Council of Sydney proposed to eliminate the alcohol-free zones in April 2016.<ref name="Barclay 2016"/>

===Azerbaijan===
Alcohol-free zone networks have been established in areas of Azerbaijan since at least 1996.<ref name="White 1996"/> These zones are located in areas such as cafes, tea shops and recreation centers.<ref name="White 1996"/>

===England===
[[File:Lickey Hills, Valley Farm Road, Rubery - Alcohol Free Zone sign.jpg|thumb|An alcohol-free zone sign at [[Lickey Hills]], [[Rubery]] in Birmingham, England]]

The first alcohol-free zone established in the United Kingdom was in [[Coventry]], England in 1988.<ref name="Bentley 2015"/> Initially, the entire city center was declared to be an alcohol-free zone.<ref name="Bentley 2015"/> The zone was later expanded to include the entire city.<ref name="Bentley 2015"/>

[[Boston, Lincolnshire]] established a street drinking ban in January 2015 to address concerns with the antisocial problems associated with public alcohol consumption.<ref name="Target 2015"/> The street drinking ban includes the town center and other areas.<ref name="Target 2015"/> Under rules of the ban, those caught drinking in these areas can be fined £100 for a first offense.<ref name="Target 2015"/> A second offense within six months after having committed a first one can be fined up to £500.<ref name="Target 2015"/> Additionally, those that are requested to stop drinking and continue to do so can receive a fine of up to £500.<ref name="Target 2015"/> In January 2015 to commemorate the event, various town council members and city police attended an event where cans of beer were poured down a city street drain.<ref name="Target 2015"/>

In 2009, [[Knowsley, Merseyside]] established alcohol-free zones in some of its parks to address problems with binge drinking-related crime and antisocial behavior.<ref name="Martin Lancer 2015"/> Drinking in licensed [[drinking establishment]]s was not affected by the zones.<ref name="Martin Lancer 2015"/>

[[Liverpool]], England has some alcohol-free zones in areas of the city.<ref name="Wright 2015"/>

===India===
The entire region of [[Attappadi]], [[Kerala]], India was established as an alcohol-free zone in 1996 by Chief Minister AK Antony.<ref name="The News Minute 2016"/> Despite the ban, alcohol has been illegally brought into the region by community residents who travel to [[Annaikatti]] to purchase liquor from [[TASMAC]] stores.<ref name="The News Minute 2016"/>

===United States===
Some parks and recreation areas in the United States are alcohol-free zones, such as in [[Johnson County, Iowa]], where alcohol consumption is allowed in some park and recreation areas but not others.<ref name="Schmidt 2016"/> For example, alcoholic beverages may be consumed in some day use areas and campgrounds in parks, but not in beach areas within some parks.<ref name="Schmidt 2016"/>

The riverfront at [[Covington, Ohio]] was established as an alcohol-free zone circa 1989 by Covington police.<ref name="Pulfer 1989"/>
