'''Carefree Black Girls''' is a cultural concept and movement that aims to increase the breadth of “alternative” representations of black women.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://madamenoire.com/522771/struggle-finding-identity-carefree-black-girl/|title=The Struggle To Be A Carefree Black Girl|last1=Jones|first1=Deja|date=2 April 2015|work=Madame Noire|access-date=30 August 2016}}</ref><ref>Mooney, Heather. "Sad Girls and Carefree Black Girls: Affect, Race,(Dis) Possession, and Protest." ''WSQ: Women's Studies Quarterly'' 46.3 (2018): 175-194.</ref> The origins of this expression can be traced to both [[Twitter]] and [[Tumblr]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.therivetermagazine.com/beyond-the-black-girl-nerds-hashtag/|title=Beyond the Black Girl Nerds Hashtag|last1=Bustos|first1=Kristina|date=10 March 2015|work=Riveter|access-date=29 August 2016}}</ref> [[Zeba Blay]] was reportedly the first person to use the expression as a hashtag on [[Twitter]] in May 2013. [[Danielle Hawkins]] soon launched a blog on Tumblr by the same name. In her article for ''[[The Root]]'', Diamond Sharp describes "carefree black girls" as an idea that, “[black women] have used to anchor expressions of individuality and whimsy in the face of the heavy [[Stereotypes of African Americans|stereotypes]] and painful realities that too often color discussions of their demographic."<ref>{{cite news|last1=Sharp|first1=Diamond|title=Why Carefree Black Girls Are Here to Stay|url=http://www.theroot.com/articles/culture/2014/08/carefree_black_girls_how_the_hashtag_turned_into_a_movement/|access-date=29 August 2016|work=The Root|date=August 9, 2014}}</ref> At ''[[Refinery29]]'', Jamala Johns said it was "a way to celebrate all things joyous and eclectic among brown ladies. Cultivated online and driven by social media, it's one telling piece of a much wider development of inspiration assembled by and for black women."<ref>{{cite news|last1=Johns|first1=Jamala|title=Carefree Black Girls - Solange, Janelle Monae|url=http://www.refinery29.com/2014/01/61614/carefree-black-girls|access-date=29 August 2016|work=Refinery29|date=January 30, 2014}}</ref> Hillary Crosley Coker, a reporter for ''[[Jezebel (website)|Jezebel]]'' provides specific examples of notable black women embodying the concept. She claims that, "ladies like [[Chiara De Blasio|Chiara de Blasio]] (with her hippie flower headband), [[Solange Knowles|Solange [Knowles]]] and her eclectic style and [[Janelle Monáe|Janelle Monae]]'s [[Afrofuturism|futurism]] are their patron saints".<ref>{{cite news|last1=Coker|first1=Hillary Crosley|title=So, What's This 'Carefree Black Girl' Thing All About?|url=http://jezebel.com/so-whats-this-carefree-black-girl-thing-all-about-1513371610|access-date=3 September 2016|work=Jezebel|date=January 31, 2014}}</ref>

== Reception ==
The "carefree black girl" movement has prompted the development of related concepts and efforts such as "carefree black boys,” a term also dubbed by Blay.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.mtv.com/news/2936762/on-carefree-black-boys/|title=On Carefree Black Boys|last=St. Félix|first=Doreen|date=September 27, 2016|work=MTV News|access-date=2017-05-28|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last1=Blay|first1=Zeba|title=How Boys Like Jaden Smith Are Redefining Black Masculinity|url=http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2015/06/05/jaden-smith-redefining-black-masculinity-_n_7514846.html|access-date=29 August 2016|work=The Huffington Post|date=5 June 2015}}</ref> Another concept that emerged was "carefree black kids" via the hashtag from ''[[Another Round (podcast)|Another Round]]'' host and ''[[Late Night with Stephen Colbert]]'' writer [[Heben Nigatu]] (#carefreeblackkids2k16).<ref>{{cite news|title=#CarefreeBlackKids2k16 offers comfort in wake of U.S. shootings|url=http://www.cbc.ca/news/trending/carefree-black-kids-1.3672103|access-date=3 September 2016|work=CBC News|date=July 9, 2016}}</ref> In July 2016, ''[[Blavity]]'' called the photos and videos posted with Nigatu's hashtag "the bright light we needed after this troubling week,” which was marked by the [[Police Brutality|state-sponsored killings]] of [[Alton Sterling]] and [[Shooting of Philando Castile|Philandro Castile]].<ref>{{cite news|last1=Mangum|first1=Trey|title=#CarefreeBlackKids2k16 is the bright light we needed after this troubling week -|url=http://blavity.com/carefreeblackkids2k16-trending-just-needed-today/|access-date=3 September 2016|work=Blavity|date=8 July 2016}}</ref>

=== Criticism ===
As the "carefree black girl" concept gained favorable recognition, it has also faced criticism.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://beyondclassicallybeautiful.com/2016/06/this-youtuber-says-shes-not-here-for-the-carefree-black-girl-movement/|title=This Youtuber Says She’s Not Here For The ‘Carefree Black Girl’ Movement|last1=BCB Team|date=14 June 2016|work=Beyond Classically Beautiful|access-date=29 August 2016}}</ref> Shamira Ibrahim, reporter for [[The Root (magazine)|''The Root'']] compares the emergence of the "carefree black girl" concept to [[Black Girl Magic|"black girl magic,"]] critiquing the term's usage as "a catch-all term that seems to run counter to the reality of being a black woman not just in America but in much of the world."<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.theroot.com/articles/culture/2016/03/why_i_m_over_the_carefree_black_girl_label/|title=Why I’m Over the ‘Carefree Black Girl’ Label|last=Shamira|first=Ibrahim|date=March 11, 2016|work=The Root|access-date=September 3, 2016}}</ref>
