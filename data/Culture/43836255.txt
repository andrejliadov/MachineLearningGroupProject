"'''Basic bitch'''" or simply "'''basic'''", also known as "'''airhead'''",<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://jezebel.com/the-united-states-of-basic-bitches-1575949216|title=The United States of Basic Bitches: A Map and Field Guide|website=Jezebel}}</ref> is a [[slang]] term in [[American popular culture]] used pejoratively to describe women who are perceived to prefer mainstream products, trends, and/or [[Pop music|music]].<ref name="inthe80s.com">{{cite web|url=http://www.inthe80s.com/glossary.shtml|title=In The 80s - Glossary of Eighties Terms|publisher=}}</ref> "Airhead" dates back to the late 1980s as a derogatory term for a stupid or unaware person, usually female.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=7tKu927c6c4C|title=Cool: The Signs and Meanings of Adolescence|first=Marcel|last=Danesi|date=1 January 1994|publisher=University of Toronto Press|via=Google Books}}</ref> "Basic bitch" originated in [[hip-hop culture]] and rose in popularity through [[rap music]], songs, blogs, and videos from 2011 to 2014.<ref name=Schreiber>{{cite web |first=Abby |last=Schreiber |date=October 16, 2014 |title=Why are 'basic bitches' taking over the zeitgeist right now? |work=[[Paper (magazine)|Paper]] |url=http://www.papermag.com/2014/10/basic_bitches_taking_over_the_zeitgeist.php |accessdate=December 24, 2014}}</ref><ref name=Alter>{{cite web |first=Charlotte |last=Alter |date=April 30, 2014 |title=How Conformity Became a Crime |work=[[Time (magazine)|Time]] |url=http://time.com/77305/how-conformity-became-a-crime/ |accessdate=December 24, 2014}}</ref>

Their male counterparts are usually termed "[[Bro culture|bro]]s",<ref>{{cite web|url=http://blogs.phoenixnewtimes.com/jackalope/2014/05/basic_bro_dating_signs.php|title=50 Signs You're Dating a Basic Bro|date=10 December 2014|work=[[Phoenix New Times]]}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://bleacherreport.com/articles/2293516-most-basic-bros-in-sports|title=Most Basic Bros in Sports|date=10 December 2014|publisher=[[Bleacher Report]]}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mtv.com/news/1972041/basic-bro-lines/|title=23 Phrases That Let You Know You're Talking To A Basic Bro|date=10 December 2014|publisher=[[MTV]]}}</ref> "[[Chad (slang)|Chads]]", or "urban preps".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.theurbangent.com/2008/06/mens-fashion-trends.html|title=Men's Fashion Trends|publisher=}}</ref> Similar labels to "basic bitch" or "airhead" in other English countries include contemporary British "[[Essex Girl]]s" and "[[Sloane ranger]]s", Irish "[[Dublin 4|D4s]]", and Australian "haul girls" known for their love of shopping for designer gear, and uploading videos of their purchases on [[YouTube]].<ref name="smh.com.au">{{cite web|url=http://www.smh.com.au/nsw/a-spotters-guide-to-the-emerging-tribes-of-sydney-20140607-39qc2.html|title=A spotter's guide to the emerging tribes of Sydney|publisher=}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://lovindublin.com/feature/19-ways-to-instantly-spot-a-basic-bitch-in-dublin|title=19 Ways To Instantly Spot A Basic Bitch In Dublin|website=lovindublin.com}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/culture/2014/mar/20/youth-subcultures-where-have-they-gone|title=Youth subcultures: what are they now?|first=Alexis|last=Petridis|date=20 March 2014|publisher=|via=www.theguardian.com}}</ref>

== History ==
===Origins===
Before the 1980s, "airhead" was general American slang for a ditzy, clumsy or stupid person.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=KT-cAQAAQBAJ&q=airhead%20preppy&pg=PA398|title=Oxford Dictionary of Modern Slang|first1=John|last1=Ayto|first2=John|last2=Simpson|date=11 February 2010|publisher=OUP Oxford|via=Google Books}}</ref> With the rise of the [[valley girl]]<ref>{{cite web|url=https://prezi.com/06z9x0lmcfgo/valley-girl-and-identity-creation/|title="Valley Girl" and identity creation|publisher=}}</ref> and [[preppy]] subculture however, the term was applied to [[cheerleader]]s<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.themycenaean.org/2011/11/cheerleaders-is-the-media-to-blame-for-stereotypes/|title=Cheerleaders: is the media to blame for stereotypes?|first1=Hailey|last1=Stephenson|first2=Senior|last2=Editor|date=14 November 2011|publisher=}}</ref> and [[nouveau riche]] or middle class hangers-on who imitated the [[uptalk]] speech<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nbcnews.com/health/body-odd/we-all-speak-valley-girls-now-f2D11722245|title=We all speak like Valley girls now - NBC News|publisher=}}</ref> and clothing of the upper class popular girls. These airheads, [[Material girl (disambiguation)|material girl]]s or [[wikt:gold digger|gold digger]]s were stereotypically viewed by their classmates as unintelligent, gossipy<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0086525/|title=Valley Girl|date=29 April 1983|publisher=|via=IMDb}}</ref> [[bimbettes]] who were interested solely in spreading rumors about their rivals and entering relationships with the wealthy jocks.<ref name="inthe80s.com"/>

===1990s and 2000s===
During the late 1990s and early 2000s, airheads began to be regarded as a distinct, middle class subculture in many suburban American high schools, although appearance-wise they initially differed little from the traditionalist upper class preps.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://archive.boston.com/ae/tv/articles/2009/07/16/let_me_school_you_about_preppies/|title=Let me school you about preppies|first=Sam|last=Allis|date=16 July 2009|publisher=|via=The Boston Globe}}</ref> At the time, many wealthy white [[jock (athlete)|jock]]s and younger [[preppies]] had begun imitating urban fashion trends, eschewing the semi-formal conservative look of the 1980s and 1990s in favor of gold [[bling]], expensive [[designer clothes]], [[sneakers (footwear)|sneakers]], dark jeans, and [[sweatpants]].<ref>[https://web.archive.org/web/20100110062908/http://www.undertheboards.com/excerpts.html Last White Superstar]. Web.archive.org (10 January 2010).</ref> Rich girls who dressed this way were known as ''[[Queen bee (sociology)|Queen Bees]]'' and their followers were known as ''plastics'', or ''airheads''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://screencrush.com/meanest-high-school-girls-movies/|title=The Meanest High School Girls in Movies|publisher=}}</ref> Members of this clique believed their designer clothing, as a manifestation of [[conspicuous consumption]], was key to being popular.<ref name="the Book of Lists for Teens">{{cite book|url=https://archive.org/details/bookoflistsforte0000chor|url-access=registration|title=the Book of Lists for Teens|first1=Sandra|last1=Choron|first2=Harry|last2=Choron|date=24 October 2002|publisher=Houghton Mifflin Harcourt|via=Internet Archive}}</ref>

===2010s===
The term ''basic bitch'' was coined in 2009<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/annehelenpetersen/basic-class-anxiety|title=What We're Really Afraid Of When We Call Someone "Basic"|website=BuzzFeed News}}</ref> by comedian [[Lil Duval|Lil' Duval]].<ref name=TheCut /><ref name=Alter /> Over the next two years, it appeared in several American rap songs. In the songs "Hard in the Paint" by [[Tyga]] and "I'm not a Human Being" by [[Lil Wayne]], the singers insist that they are not basic bitches, while in the song "Basic Bitch" by [[The Game (rapper)|the Game]], the singer warns others to avoid basic bitches because they are fake.<ref name=TheCut>{{cite web|url=http://nymag.com/thecut/2014/04/basic-bitch-who-is-she.html|title= The 'Basic Bitch': Who Is She?|work=NYMag.com|author= Maggie Lange |date=April 10, 2014|accessdate= September 15, 2014}}</ref>

In 2011, rapper [[Kreayshawn]] debuted her song "Gucci Gucci", which included the chorus: "Gucci Gucci, Louis Louis, Fendi Fendi, Prada ... basic bitches wear that shit so I don't even bother." In 2014 [[CollegeHumor]] released a parody video of a wife being diagnosed by a doctor as a "basic bitch", to the horror of her husband.<ref name=Vox>{{cite web|url=https://www.vox.com/2014/4/22/5636156/basic-a-beginners-guide-to-everyones-favorite-new-insult|title= "Basic": a beginner's guide to everyone's favorite new insult|author= Alex Abad-Santos |work=Vox.com|date=April 22, 2014|accessdate= September 15, 2014}}</ref><ref name=Schreiber /><ref name=Alter />

== Interpretations and criticism ==
Referring to an object or a person as "basic" has a variety of connotations. When used to refer to people, it can mean a criticism of shallow [[Economic materialism|materialism]];{{citation needed|date=February 2015}} in songs that use the term, popular luxury brands like [[Gucci]] and [[Prada]] are referenced to suggest that the people who wear them are buying, rather than earning, their fashionability and [[social status]].<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.bustle.com/articles/39504-how-to-spot-the-basic-bitch-a-field-guide|title=How to Spot the Basic Bitch|first=Lucia|last=Peters|website=Bustle}}</ref> Decrying the basic bitch's love of bland, boring products like [[Ugg boots]] and [[Starbucks]] [[pumpkin spice latte]]s is a rhetorical technique that allows the singer to appear cooler by comparison.<ref name=AmericanReader>{{cite web|url=http://theamericanreader.com/the-life-and-death-of-american-slang-part-ii-bae-and-basic-bitch/|author=Michael Reid Roberts|work=The American Reader|title=The Life and Death of American Slang, Part II: Bae and Basic Bitch|accessdate=September 15, 2014}}</ref> 

In an article in ''[[The Guardian]]'' titled, "Why I'm proud to be a 'basic bitch'", journalist Daisy Buchanan has criticized the cultural trend of using "basic bitch" as an insult, pointing out that those who call out other women for being basic bitches are "dismissing all cultural feminine signifiers" and "make assumptions about a woman's interests and habits based on her sex"<ref name=Guardian>{{cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2014/apr/14/proud-to-be-basic-bitch|author=Daisy Buchanan|work=The Guardian|title=Why I'm proud to be a 'basic bitch'|date=April 14, 2014|accessdate=September 15, 2014}}</ref> Widespread usage of the term to mock the behavior and interests of girlfriends or wives "conforms to the most bland and uncreative stereotypes of [[Late capitalism|late capitalist]] femininity" and suggests a misogynistic attitude toward all women, according to Michael Reid Roberts in an article in ''The American Reader''.<ref name=AmericanReader />

== In the media ==
Since the 1980s, the airhead or popular clique have often been cast as the antagonists in high school and teen movies.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://collegecandy.com/2014/07/14/the-15-most-basic-bitch-movies-in-history-ranked/|title=The 15 Most Basic Bitch Movies In History Ranked|first=Emerald|last=Pellot|date=14 July 2014|publisher=}}</ref> Examples include ''[[Valley Girl (1983 film)|Valley Girl]]'', ''[[The Clique (film)|The Clique]]'', ''[[Heathers]]'', ''[[Girl Fight]]'', ''[[A Cinderella Story]]'', ''[[Odd Girl Out]]'', ''[[Frenemies (film)|Frenemies]]'', ''[[Pretty in Pink]]'', ''[[Mean Girls]]'', and ''[[Clueless (film)|Clueless]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.theedgesusu.co.uk/news/2015/07/19/nostalgic-news-clueless-released-20-years-ago-today/|title=Nostalgic News: Clueless released 20 years ago today|date=18 July 2015|publisher=}}</ref>
