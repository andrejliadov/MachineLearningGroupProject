{{Short description|Lagrangian used in classical electrodynamics}}
The '''Darwin Lagrangian''' (named after [[Charles Galton Darwin]], grandson of [[Charles Darwin|the naturalist]]) describes the interaction to order <math>\frac{v^2}{c^2}</math> between two charged particles in a vacuum and is given by<ref>{{cite book |author=Jackson, John D.|title=Classical Electrodynamics (3rd ed.)|publisher=Wiley|year=1998|isbn=047130932X}} pp. 596-598</ref>

:<math> L = L_\text{f} + L_\text{int}, </math>

where the free particle [[Lagrangian mechanics|Lagrangian]] is

:<math> L_\text{f} = \frac{1}{2} m_1 v_1^2 + \frac{1}{8c^2} m_1 v_1^4 + \frac{1}{2} m_2 v_2^2 + \frac{1}{8c^2} m_2 v_2^4, </math>

and the interaction Lagrangian is

:<math> L_\text{int} = L_\text{C} + L_\text{D}, </math>

where the [[Coulomb force|Coulomb interaction]] is

:<math>L_\text{C} = -\frac{q_1 q_2}{r}, </math>

and the [[Charles Galton Darwin|Darwin]] interaction is

:<math> L_\text{D} = \frac{q_1 q_2}{r} \frac{1}{2c^2} \mathbf v_1 \cdot \left[\mathbf 1 + \mathbf{\hat r} \mathbf{\hat r}\right] \cdot \mathbf v_2. </math>

Here ''q''<sub>1</sub> and ''q''<sub>2</sub> are the charges on particles 1 and 2 respectively, ''m''<sub>1</sub> and ''m''<sub>2</sub> are the masses of the particles, '''v'''<sub>1</sub> and '''v'''<sub>2</sub> are the velocities of the particles, ''c'' is the [[speed of light]], '''r''' is the vector between the two particles, and <math>\hat{\mathbf r}</math> is the [[unit vector]] in the direction of '''r'''.

The free Lagrangian is the [[Taylor expansion]] of free Lagrangian of two relativistic particles to second order in ''v''. The Darwin interaction term is due to one particle reacting to the [[magnetic field]] generated by the other particle. If higher-order terms in ''v''/''c'' are retained, then the field degrees of freedom must be taken into account, and the interaction can no longer be taken to be instantaneous between the particles. In that case [[Retarded potential|retardation]] effects must be accounted for.

==Derivation in vacuum==
The relativistic interaction Lagrangian for a particle with charge q interacting with an electromagnetic field is<ref>Jackson, pp. 580-581.</ref>

:<math> L_\text{int} = -q\Phi + {q\over c} \mathbf u \cdot \mathbf A, </math>

where '''u''' is the relativistic velocity of the particle. The first term on the right generates the Coulomb interaction. The second term generates the Darwin interaction.

The [[vector potential]] in the [[Coulomb gauge]] is described by<ref>Jackson, p. 242.</ref> ([[Gaussian units]])

:<math> \nabla^2 \mathbf A - {1\over c^2} {\partial^2 \mathbf A \over \partial t^2} = -{4\pi \over c} \mathbf J_t</math>

where the transverse current '''J'''<sub>''t''</sub> is the [[solenoidal field|solenoidal current]] (see [[Helmholtz decomposition]]) generated by a second particle. The [[divergence]] of the transverse current is zero.

The current generated by the second particle is

:<math>  \mathbf J = q_2 \mathbf v_2 \delta \left( \mathbf r - \mathbf r_2 \right),</math>

which has a [[Fourier transform]]

:<math>  \mathbf J\left( \mathbf k \right) \equiv  \int d^3r \exp\left( -i\mathbf k \cdot \mathbf r \right) \mathbf J\left( \mathbf r \right) = q_2 \mathbf v_2 \exp\left( -i\mathbf k \cdot \mathbf r_2 \right).</math>

The transverse component of the current is

:<math>\mathbf J_t\left( \mathbf k \right) = q_2 \left[ \mathbf 1 - \mathbf{\hat k} \mathbf{\hat k} \right] \cdot \mathbf v_2 \exp\left( -i\mathbf k \cdot \mathbf r_2 \right).</math>

It is easily verified that

:<math>\mathbf k \cdot \mathbf J_t\left( \mathbf k \right) = 0,</math>

which must be true if the divergence of the transverse current is zero. We see that

:<math>\mathbf J_t\left( \mathbf k \right) </math>

is the component of the Fourier transformed current perpendicular to '''k'''.

From the equation for the vector potential, the Fourier transform of the vector potential is

:<math>  
 \mathbf A \left( \mathbf k \right)
 = {4\pi \over c} {q_2\over k^2} \left[ \mathbf 1 - \mathbf{\hat k} \mathbf{\hat k} \right] \cdot \mathbf v_2
\exp\left( -i\mathbf k \cdot \mathbf r_2 \right)
  </math>

where we have kept only the lowest order term in v/c.

The inverse Fourier transform of the vector potential is

:<math>\mathbf A \left( \mathbf r \right)  
=\int { d^3 k \over \left ( 2 \pi \right ) ^3 } \; \mathbf A \left( \mathbf k \right) \;{ \exp \left ( i \mathbf k \cdot \mathbf r_1 \right )  } 
= {q_2\over 2c} {1 \over  r } \left[\mathbf 1 + \mathbf{\hat r} \mathbf{\hat r}\right] \cdot \mathbf v_2</math>

where

:<math>\mathbf r = \mathbf r_1 - \mathbf r_2 </math>

(see [[Common integrals in quantum field theory#Transverse potential with mass|Common integrals in quantum field theory]]).

The Darwin interaction term in the Lagrangian is then

::{|cellpadding="2" style="border:2px solid #ccccff"
|
<math>L_{\rm D} = 
{q_1 q_2\over r} {1 \over 2c^2  } \mathbf v_1 \cdot 
\left[\mathbf 1 + \mathbf{\hat r} \mathbf{\hat r}\right]
\cdot \mathbf v_2  </math>
|}

where again we kept only the lowest order term in v/c.

==Lagrangian equations of motion==
The [[equations of motion|equation of motion]] for one of the particles is

:<math>  {d \over dt} {\partial  \over \partial \mathbf v_1} L\left( \mathbf r_1 , \mathbf v_1 \right) = \nabla_1 L\left( \mathbf r_1 , \mathbf v_1 \right)  </math>
:<math>  {d \mathbf p_1 \over dt} = \nabla_1 L\left( \mathbf r_1 , \mathbf v_1 \right) </math>

where '''p'''<sub>1</sub> is the [[momentum]] of the particle.

===Free particle===
The equation of motion for a free particle neglecting interactions between the two particles is

:<math>{d \over dt} \left[ \left( 1   + {1\over 2} { v_1^2\over c^2 } \right)m_1\mathbf v_1 \right]=0</math>
:<math> \mathbf p_1 = \left( 1   + {1\over 2} { v_1^2\over c^2 } \right)m_1\mathbf v_1 </math>

===Interacting particles===
For interacting particles, the equation of motion becomes

:<math>  {d \over dt} \left[ \left( 1   + {1\over 2} { v_1^2\over c^2 } \right)m_1\mathbf v_1 +{q_1\over c}\mathbf A\left( \mathbf r_1 \right) \right]=
-\nabla {q_1 q_2 \over r}
+\nabla \left[ {q_1q_2 \over  r }{1\over 2c^2}
\mathbf v_1\cdot  
\left[\mathbf 1 + \mathbf{\hat r} \mathbf{\hat r}\right]
\cdot\mathbf v_2 \right]  </math>

::{|cellpadding="2" style="border:2px solid #ccccff"
|
<math> {d \mathbf{p}_1\over dt} 
= {q_1 q_2 \over r^2}{\hat{\mathbf r}}
+{q_1 q_2 \over r^2}{1\over 2c^2} 
\left\{ \mathbf v_1 \left( { {\hat{\mathbf r}}\cdot \mathbf v_2} \right) 
+  \mathbf v_2 \left( { {\hat{\mathbf r}}\cdot \mathbf v_1}\right)
- {\hat{\mathbf r}} \left[ \mathbf v_1 \cdot \left( \mathbf 1 +3 {\hat{\mathbf r}}{\hat{\mathbf r}}\right)\cdot \mathbf v_2\right]
 \right\}  </math>
|}

:<math> \mathbf p_1 =
\left( 1   + {1\over 2} { v_1^2\over c^2 } \right)m_1\mathbf v_1
+{q_1\over c}\mathbf A\left( \mathbf r_1 \right)</math>

:<math>\mathbf A \left( \mathbf r_1 \right)  =  {q_2\over 2c} {1 \over  r }  
\left[\mathbf 1 + \mathbf{\hat r} \mathbf{\hat r}\right]
\cdot \mathbf v_2  </math>

:<math>\mathbf r =\mathbf r_1 - \mathbf r_2</math>

==Hamiltonian for two particles in a vacuum==
The Darwin [[Hamiltonian mechanics|Hamiltonian]] for two particles in a vacuum is related to the Lagrangian by a [[Legendre transformation]]

:<math>  H = \mathbf p_1 \cdot \mathbf v_1 + \mathbf p_2 \cdot \mathbf v_2 - L.</math>

The Hamiltonian becomes

{|cellpadding="2" style="border:2px solid #ccccff"
|
<math> H\left( \mathbf r_1 , \mathbf p_1 ,\mathbf r_2 , \mathbf p_2 \right)=
\left( 1   - {1\over 4} { p_1^2\over m_1^2 c^2 } \right){ p_1^2 \over 2 m_1}
\; + \; \left( 1   - {1\over 4} { p_2^2\over m_2^2 c^2 } \right){ p_2^2 \over 2 m_2}
\; + \; {q_1 q_2 \over r }
\; - \; {q_1q_2 \over  r }{1\over 2m_1 m_2 c^2}
\mathbf p_1\cdot  
\left[\mathbf 1 + \mathbf{\hat r} \mathbf{\hat r}\right]
\cdot\mathbf p_2
  .</math>
|}

==Hamiltonian equations of motion==
The Hamiltonian equations of motion are

:<math>  \mathbf v_1 = {\partial H \over \partial \mathbf p_1}  </math>

and

:<math>  {d \mathbf p_1 \over dt} = -\nabla_1 H </math>

which yield

:<math>  \mathbf v_1  =
\left( 1- {1\over 2} {p_1^2 \over m_1^2 c^2}  \right) {\mathbf p_1 \over m_1}
- {q_1 q_2\over 2m_1m_2 c^2} {1 \over  r }  
\left[\mathbf 1 + \mathbf{\hat r} \mathbf{\hat r}\right]
\cdot \mathbf p_2</math>

and

{|cellpadding="2" style="border:2px solid #ccccff"
|
<math>  {d \mathbf p_1\over dt} = {q_1 q_2 \over r^2}{\hat{\mathbf r}}
\; + \; {q_1 q_2 \over r^2}{1\over 2m_1 m_2 c^2} 
\left\{ \mathbf p_1 \left( { {\hat{\mathbf r}}\cdot \mathbf p_2} \right) 
+  \mathbf p_2 \left( { {\hat{\mathbf r}}\cdot \mathbf p_1}\right)
- {\hat{\mathbf r}} \left[ \mathbf p_1 \cdot \left( \mathbf 1 +3 {\hat{\mathbf r}}{\hat{\mathbf r}}\right)\cdot \mathbf p_2\right]
 \right\}  </math>
|}
Note that the quantum mechanical [[Breit equation]] originally used the Darwin Lagrangian with the Darwin Hamiltonian as its classical starting point though the Breit equation would be better vindicated by the [[Wheeler–Feynman absorber theory]] and better yet [[quantum electrodynamics]].
