'''''The Concord Review: A Quarterly Review of Essays by Students of History''''' is an [[academic journal]] dedicated to publishing the research papers of [[high school]] students.<ref>{{cite book |title=Your Name in Print: A Teen's Guide to Publishing for Fun, Profit and Academic Success |last=Harper |first=Elizabeth |authorlink= |author2=Timothy Harper |year=2005 |publisher=St. Martin's Griffin |location= |isbn=978-0-312-33759-9 |page=[https://archive.org/details/yournameinprintt00harp/page/166 166] |url=https://archive.org/details/yournameinprintt00harp/page/166 |url-access=registration }}</ref> Established in 1987 by [[William H. Fitzhugh (teacher)|William H. Fitzhugh]], a [[Massachusetts]] educator dismayed with the "[[dumbing down]]" of writing standards in American secondary schools, publication in the ''Review'' is considered among the most prestigious awards for high school students.<ref name=Dillon>{{cite news |title=Journal Showcases Dying Art of the Research Paper |author=Sam Dillon |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2011/01/08/education/08research.html |newspaper=The New York Times |date=7 January 2011 |accessdate=8 January 2011}}</ref>

Issued quarterly, the journal publishes research monographs on history topics from high school students from any country, as long as they are in English. Submissions are typically 4000 to 5500 words long, and must be accompanied by a subscription fee (ranging from $70-$150) to the journal in order to be considered.

== Related activities ==
In addition to publishing the journal, The Concord Review, Inc. engages in a number of other projects. Five outstanding essays per year are awarded the [[Ralph Waldo Emerson]] Prize of about $3000.<ref name = Rosemary>{{cite book |title=Teaching the gifted in an inclusion classroom: Activities that work |last=Callard-Szulgit |first=Rosemary S. |authorlink= |year=2005 |publisher=Rowman & Littlefield Education |location= |isbn=978-1-57886-185-9 |page=142 |url= }}</ref> Fitzhugh also founded the National History Club, with chapters in hundreds of American high schools. Also, The Concord Review, Inc. operates a service called the National Writing Board, which for a fee of $100 will evaluate student writing and forward the results to college admissions committees.<ref name = Proctor/>

== Reception and support ==
Among others, the ''Review'' has been supported by Boston-based multimillionaire [[John Abele]].<ref name="Dillon" />

Having a paper accepted for publication by ''The Concord Review'' is viewed very favorably by [[University and college admissions|university admissions]] committees,<ref name="Proctor">{{cite book |title=The College Hook: Packaging Yourself to Win the College Admissions Game |last=Proctor |first=Pam |authorlink= |year=2007 |publisher=Center Street |location= |isbn=978-1-931722-81-0 |pages=272 |url= }}</ref> with the Dean of Admissions for [[Harvard University]] considering it equivalent to winning a national mathematics contest. [[Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr.]], taped a video testimonial praising the ''Review'' for providing a scholarly venue for high school students.<ref name="Dillon" />
