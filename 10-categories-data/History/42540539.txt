The '''Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American Culture and History''' is a [[special library]] within the [[Atlanta-Fulton Public Library System]], located in Atlanta's [[Sweet Auburn]] Historic District.<ref name="Auburn">{{cite web |title=About Us |url= http://www.afpls.org/aboutaarl|publisher=Atlanta-Fulton Public Library System. afpls.org |access-date=August 9, 2019}}</ref> The Auburn Avenue Research Library "is the first library in the Southeast to offer specialized reference and archival collections dedicated to the study and research of African American culture and history and of other peoples of African descent."<ref name="Wesley">{{cite web |first= Wesley |last=Chenault |url= http://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/articles/education/auburn-avenue-research-library-african-american-culture-and-history |date=February 13, 2009 |title=Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American Culture and History |publisher=New Georgia Encyclopedia. georgiaencyclopedia.org |access-date=August 9, 2019}}</ref>

"Occupying 50,000 square feet, the Auburn Avenue Research Library's four-story, red-brick-and-black-granite structure is actually three buildings in one: a library research area containing general reference books and materials, study areas, and a reading room; a public section housing exhibit cases, general reference materials, and a main reading room; and an archive that includes a core of library stacks running through the center of the building on the second and third floors."<ref>{{cite journal|last=Chepesiuk|first=R.|title=Schomburg of the South: the Auburn Research Library|journal=American Libraries|date=1996|volume=27|pages=38–40}}</ref>

The library is open to the public.<ref name="libcard">{{cite web |title=Access to the Collections|url= http://www.afpls.org/aarl-access-to-the-collections|publisher=Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American Culture and History}}</ref> Also, "all researchers wishing to use archival collections are encouraged to make an appointment with an Archives Division staff person prior to visiting the Research Library."<ref name="libcard" />

"In 2001, the Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American Culture and History received a Governor's Award in the Humanities."<ref name="Wesley" />

The library was temporarily closed for about two years but re-opened in 2016 after $20 million worth of renovations.<ref>https://www.wabe.org/auburn-avenue-research-library-reopens-after-20m-renovation/</ref><ref>https://atlantadailyworld.com/2014/01/09/auburn-avenue-research-library-to-close-for-renovations-and-expansion/</ref>

==History==

The Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American History and culture opened in May 1994 in its current location.<ref name="Auburn" />
"Its core collection was formed at the Auburn Branch of the Carnegie Library of Atlanta."<ref name="Auburn" /> "This branch, opened in 1921, was Atlanta's first public library branch for African Americans."<ref name="Auburn" /> "Due to [[Jim Crow laws]], African Americans were denied public library service which began in 1902."<ref name="Auburn" />

"From 1921-1959, the Auburn Avenue Research Library provided educational and community programming and amassed its core collection called the Negro History Collection."<ref name="Auburn" /> "The collection grew to include bound copies of magazines, newspapers, and scholarly journals by, for, and about African Americans, including the Atlanta Daily World, Crisis, Journal of Negro Education, and Negro History Bulletin."<ref name="Auburn" /> The library closed in 1959.<ref name="Auburn" /> After the library's closure, the Negro History Collection moved to the West Hunter Branch.<ref name="Auburn" /> "This branch was built in 1949 to accommodate the demands for expanded public library service from the increasing numbers of African Americans who had migrated to Atlanta's west side."<ref name="Auburn" />

The collection moved again in 1970, to "where Central Library now stands at One Margaret Mitchell Square."<ref name="Auburn" /> "One year later, the collection was named the '''[[Samuel Woodrow Williams|Samuel W. Williams]] Collection on Black America''', in honor of the Atlanta-based educator, theologian, and philosopher."<ref name="Auburn" /> "The collection remained at the main library until 1994, and was then moved to the newly built Auburn Avenue Research Library."<ref name="Auburn" />

"After fifteen years of operation, the Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American Culture and History had outgrown its space."<ref name="Auburn" /> "On November 4, 2008, Fulton County voters approved a library bond [[referendum]], which included funds to enhance and expand the facility."<ref name="Auburn" />

==Divisions==

===Reference & Research===
"The Samuel W. Williams Collection forms the cornerstone of the reference and research division, which maintains a variety of textual and [[microform]] records, a noncirculating library of secondary sources, and a broad array of Web-accessible research databases for the study of African American culture and history and the [[African diaspora|African Diaspora]]."<ref name="Auburn" />

===Archives===
"The Archives division preserves and makes available unique historical records of enduring value related primarily to African American culture and history, with a concentration on local Atlanta history."<ref name="Auburn" /> "These primary sources include not only textual and special media records ([[cartography|cartographic]] records, graphic arts, still photographs, sound recordings, and moving images) but also art and artifacts, microforms, rare book collections, and textiles."<ref name="Auburn" />

===Program===
"The Program division supports the library's mission and serves the public through book discussions and readings, exhibitions, film screenings, lectures, seminars, tours, and workshops."<ref name="Auburn" /> "Through its cultural, educational, and scholarly programming—which is local, national, and transnational in scope—the program division helps interpret and highlight the institution's rich collections and provides outreach to the general public, as well as to Atlanta's academic community."<ref name="Auburn" />

==Collections==

The sections of the Auburn Avenue Research Library's collection that are contained in the Archives Division include "the '''Art and Artifacts Collection''' which contains art and artifacts by and about peoples of African heritage throughout the world."<ref name="Collections">{{cite web |title=Collections|url=http://www.afpls.org/aarlcollections|publisher=Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American Culture and History}}</ref> "The collection contains contemporary works by African American artists, including works by [[Romare Bearden]], [[Charles White (artist)|Charles White]], [[James Van Der Zee]] and [[Ed Dwight]]."<ref name="Collections" /> "The Archives Division also includes the '''Archives and Manuscripts Collection''', which focuses on the history, literature, politics, and culture of [[African Americans in Atlanta|peoples of African descent in Atlanta]], the Southeast, the U.S. and the world."<ref name="Collections" />

Other collections include "the '''Ephemeral Collection''' which contains resources in the following categories: calendars, [[dust jacket]]s, family genealogical booklets, flags, maps, postcards, posters, sheet music, slave documents, yearbooks, [[vertical file]]s, educational kits, black history month essays, funeral programs, coins, comic books, playbills, typescripts, buttons, recipes, autographs and stamps."<ref name="Collections" />

The '''Moving Image and Recorded Sound Collection''' "provides audiovisual resources which document and interpret African American experiences in the city of Atlanta, the state of Georgia, the southeastern region of the U.S. and throughout the nation."<ref name="Collections" />

The '''Oral History Collection''' "complements the Moving Image and Recorded Sound Collection."<ref name="Collections" /> "Preserved on videocassette tapes, audio cassette tapes and transcripts, and digital formats, this expanding collection seeks to enhance and make available collected oral histories of historical and significant cultural organizations, individuals, institutions and communities."<ref name="Collections" />

The '''Pamphlet Collection''' "includes works published before 1865 in the United States; first editions by African American authors before 1930 and during the [[Harlem Renaissance]] and works published before 1901 in Europe, Africa, South America and the Caribbean, and publications of Black private presses."<ref name="Collections" />

The '''Photographs and Prints Collection''' "contains both documentary and fine art photographs which document the history and culture of peoples of African descent as well as the work of photographers of African descent."<ref name="Collections" />

The '''Rare Periodical and Newspaper Collection''' "contains mainly African American publications. A small percentage of the collection is composed of non-African American publications."<ref name="Collections" /> "The collection includes articles, photos, illustrations, newspapers and periodicals in bound volumes."<ref name="Collections" />

'''Digital Collections'''

In 2010 the Auburn Avenue Research Library on African American Culture and History (AARL) and the [[Digital Library of Georgia]] (DLG) collaborated to digitize eleven archival collections.  AARL and DLG received funds from the [[National Historical Publications and Records Commission]] (NHPRC) to make Web-accessible late nineteenth- and mid-twentieth-century [[manuscript]] collections "that document the historical development of education for African Americans, primarily in the South, from the early 1860s to the early 1950s." [http://www.afpls.org/aarl Eleven collections] were digitized and are now available via the library website.<ref>http://www.afpls.org/aarl</ref>

The Reference and Research Division "contains the '''print, digital and microform collections''' which includes an impressive list of Africana resources that provide unlimited access to full-text articles from current and past issues of scholarly Africana journals, popular African American magazines and international African journals."<ref name="Collections" />

The "strengths of the (Auburn Avenue Research Library's) collection are Southern [[Civil Rights Movement]], Southern African American Literature and History, General African and African American History and Literature, Atlanta and Georgia Vertical File Collections, Current and Historic African American Journals, Current and Historic African American Newspapers, Africana Children's Literature Collection, The [[Coretta Scott King Award]] Winning Children's Titles 1969 to current, Africana Cinema and Documentary Film Collections, and Subject Bibliographies highlighting AARL resources."<ref name="Collections" />
