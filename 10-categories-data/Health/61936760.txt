== Shift strategies ==
Broadly, “shift” strategies encompass a variety of cognitive and [[Emotional self-regulation|emotion self-regulation]] approaches that individuals use to deal with [[Stress (biology)|stress]], including [[cognitive restructuring]], reframing, reappraisal, and acceptance strategies, which change the meaning of a stressor or reduce its emotional impact.<ref name=":0" /> These shift strategies particularly focus on changing one's response to a stressor, instead of attempting to change the situation or stressor itself.<ref name=":0" /><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Heckhausen|first=J.|last2=Schulz|first2=R.|date=1995|title=A life-span theory of control|journal=Psychological Review|volume=102|issue=2|pages=284–304|doi=10.1037/0033-295x.102.2.284|issn=0033-295X|pmid=7740091}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Heckhausen|first=Jutta|last2=Wrosch|first2=Carsten|last3=Schulz|first3=Richard|date=2010|title=A motivational theory of life-span development|journal=Psychological Review|volume=117|issue=1|pages=32–60|doi=10.1037/a0017668|issn=1939-1471|pmc=2820305|pmid=20063963}}</ref> As shift strategies depend more on internal processes (self-control and regulation), than external resources, it is hypothesized that shift strategies may be particularly adaptive responses to the chronic, uncontrollable stressors that are associated with low [[socioeconomic status]].

== Persist strategies ==
According to Chen and Miller, “persist” strategies are any strategies that help individuals to maintain [[optimism]] about the future, create [[Meaning-making|meaning]] from their experiences of challenge and hardship, and persist “with strength in the face of adversity.”<ref name=":0" />

== Measurement ==
To evaluate the combination of shift-and-persist strategy use, distinct "shift” and “persist” constructs were initially measured separately by using multiple, self-report measures of reappraisal, emotional reactivity, and future orientation in early research on this model.<ref name="Chen 2012 178–186"/>

In 2015, Chen and colleagues published the Shift-and-Persist Scale, which is a combined self-report measure that assesses both shift and persist strategies.<ref name=":1">{{Cite journal|last=Chen|first=Edith|last2=McLean|first2=Kate C.|last3=Miller|first3=Gregory E.|date=2015|title=Shift-and-Persist Strategies: Associations With Socioeconomic Status and the Regulation of Inflammation Among Adolescents and Their Parents|journal=Psychosomatic Medicine|language=en|volume=77|issue=4|pages=371–382|doi=10.1097/PSY.0000000000000157|pmid=26167560|pmc=5890430|issn=0033-3174}}</ref> The Shift-and-Persist Scale has been validated for use with adults and teenagers.<ref name=":1" /> The questionnaire asks respondents to rate how well 14 statements about various approaches to dealing with life stressors apply to them on a 1-4 scale.<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=http://sparqtools.org/mobility-measure/shift-and-persist-teen-adult/|title=Shift and Persist (Teen, Adult) {{!}} SPARQTools|last=University|first=© Stanford|last2=Stanford|website=sparqtools.org|language=en|access-date=2019-11-08|last3=California 94305}}</ref> Out of the 14 items on the measure, 4 assess a respondent's use of shift strategies, 4 load onto persist strategies, and 6 items are non-relevant distractors that are ignored during scoring. When scoring the Shift-and-Persist Scale, one item (#4) is reverse-scored. This scale is publicly available online.<ref name=":2" />

A simplified 5-item Shift-and-Persist scale has also been published for use with younger children and adolescents (ages 9–15).<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|url=http://sparqtools.org/mobility-measure/shift-and-persist-child/|title=Shift and Persist (Child) {{!}} SPARQTools|last=University|first=© Stanford|last2=Stanford|website=sparqtools.org|language=en|access-date=2019-11-08|last3=California 94305}}</ref> Total scores on this version of the Shift-and-Persist Scale range from 0-20, such that higher scores are indicative of greater use of shift-and-persist strategies. This scale is also publicly available online and has been previously used in research with children from kindergarten through 8th grade.<ref name=":3" /><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Kallem|first=Stacey|last2=Carroll‐Scott|first2=Amy|last3=Rosenthal|first3=Lisa|last4=Chen|first4=Edith|last5=Peters|first5=Susan M.|last6=McCaslin|first6=Catherine|last7=Ickovics|first7=Jeannette R.|date=2013|title=Shift-and-persist: A protective factor for elevated BMI among low-socioeconomic-status children|journal=Obesity|language=en|volume=21|issue=9|pages=1759–1763|doi=10.1002/oby.20195|issn=1930-739X|pmc=4325991|pmid=23671041}}</ref>

== Proposed mechanisms ==

=== Reduction of the harmful biological effects of stress ===

=== Enhancement of adaptive biological stress-recovery systems ===
Another alternative, but not mutually exclusive hypothesis, is that shift-and-persist strategies affect health by increasing or up-regulating biological responses that enhance stress recovery and [[Psychological resilience|resilience]]. In particular, the [[Parasympathetic nervous system|parasympathetic nervous system's]] functioning may be enhanced by shift-and-persist response tendencies.<ref name=":0" /> [[Emotional self-regulation|Emotion regulation]] abilities that are consistent with shift-coping have been linked to greater [[parasympathetic nervous system]] functioning at rest, as indexed by higher levels of high-frequency [[heart rate variability]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Appelhans|first=Bradley M.|last2=Luecken|first2=Linda J.|date=2006|title=Heart Rate Variability as an Index of Regulated Emotional Responding|journal=Review of General Psychology|volume=10|issue=3|pages=229–240|doi=10.1037/1089-2680.10.3.229|issn=1089-2680|url=https://semanticscholar.org/paper/66b5c158e99b9bb6a1e971f2c6b6732767e0c0dc}}</ref> Further, the [[parasympathetic nervous system]] is highly integrated with, and may contribute to the down-regulation of [[hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis]] and [[Immune response|immune]] system stress responses that influence [[allostatic load]] over time.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=THAYER|first=J. F|last2=STERNBERG|first2=E.|date=2006-11-01|title=Beyond Heart Rate Variability: Vagal Regulation of Allostatic Systems|journal=Annals of the New York Academy of Sciences|volume=1088|issue=1|pages=361–372|doi=10.1196/annals.1366.014|pmid=17192580|issn=0077-8923|bibcode=2006NYASA1088..361T}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Tracey|first=Kevin J.|date=2009|title=Reflex control of immunity|journal=Nature Reviews. Immunology|volume=9|issue=6|pages=418–428|doi=10.1038/nri2566|issn=1474-1741|pmc=4535331|pmid=19461672}}</ref> Although [[parasympathetic nervous system]] activity is correlated with aspects of shift-and-persist coping, it is not yet established that the use of these strategies actually increases [[parasympathetic nervous system]] activity.

The [[oxytocin]] system has also been identified as another potential mechanism by which shift-and-persist strategies could influence health outcomes.<ref name=":0" /> [[Oxytocin]] is a hormone that has been linked to a wide range of positive social and emotional functions and can be used to effectively attenuate [[hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis]] and sympathetic nervous system responses to stress.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Ditzen|first=Beate|last2=Schaer|first2=Marcel|last3=Gabriel|first3=Barbara|last4=Bodenmann|first4=Guy|last5=Ehlert|first5=Ulrike|last6=Heinrichs|first6=Markus|date=2009-05-01|title=Intranasal oxytocin increases positive communication and reduces cortisol levels during couple conflict|journal=Biological Psychiatry|volume=65|issue=9|pages=728–731|doi=10.1016/j.biopsych.2008.10.011|issn=1873-2402|pmid=19027101}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Ross|first=Heather E.|last2=Young|first2=Larry J.|date=2009-10-01|title=Oxytocin and the neural mechanisms regulating social cognition and affiliative behavior|journal=Frontiers in Neuroendocrinology|series=Hormones & Social Behavior|volume=30|issue=4|pages=534–547|doi=10.1016/j.yfrne.2009.05.004|pmid=19481567|pmc=2748133|issn=0091-3022}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Gutkowska|first=Jolanta|last2=Jankowski|first2=Marek|date=2008|title=Oxytocin revisited: It is also a cardiovascular hormone|journal=Journal of the American Society of Hypertension: JASH|volume=2|issue=5|pages=318–325|doi=10.1016/j.jash.2008.04.004|issn=1933-1711|pmid=20409913}}</ref> However, there is little research examining the interplay between shift-and-persist strategy use and the [[oxytocin]] system.

== Research support for associations with health ==
Since 2012, integrative research groups concerned with clinical [[health psychology]], [[social psychology]], [[psychoneuroimmunology]], and [[public health]] have begun to evaluate the relationships postulated by the shift-and-persist model. The majority of empirical studies on this topic test whether shift-and-persist strategies are associated with differential health outcomes in low vs. high [[socioeconomic status]] samples.

Thus far, high levels of shift-and-persist strategy use have been linked to:

* Lower total [[allostatic load]] in adults who grew up in low, but not high, [[socioeconomic status]] households.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Chen|first=Edith|last2=Miller|first2=Gregory E.|last3=Lachman|first3=Margie E.|last4=Gruenewald|first4=Tara L.|last5=Seeman|first5=Teresa E.|date=2012|title=Protective factors for adults from low-childhood socioeconomic circumstances: the benefits of shift-and-persist for allostatic load|journal=Psychosomatic Medicine|volume=74|issue=2|pages=178–186|doi=10.1097/PSY.0b013e31824206fd|issn=1534-7796|pmc=3273596|pmid=22286848}}</ref>
* Lower [[body mass index]] in children from low, but not high, [[socioeconomic status]] families.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Kallem|first=Stacey|last2=Carroll-Scott|first2=Amy|last3=Rosenthal|first3=Lisa|last4=Chen|first4=Edith|last5=Peters|first5=Susan M.|last6=McCaslin|first6=Catherine|last7=Ickovics|first7=Jeannette R.|date=2013|title=Shift-and-persist: A protective factor for elevated BMI among low-socioeconomic-status children|journal=Obesity|volume=21|issue=9|pages=1759–63|doi=10.1002/oby.20195|pmid=23671041|pmc=4325991|issn=1930-7381}}</ref>
* Reduced low-grade [[inflammation]] in adolescents (and parents) from low [[socioeconomic status]] families.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Chen|first=Edith|last2=McLean|first2=Kate C.|last3=Miller|first3=Gregory E.|date=2015|title=Shift-and-Persist Strategies|journal=Psychosomatic Medicine|volume=77|issue=4|pages=371–382|doi=10.1097/psy.0000000000000157|pmid=26167560|pmc=5890430|issn=0033-3174}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Chen|first=Edith|last2=Lee|first2=William K.|last3=Cavey|first3=Lisa|last4=Ho|first4=Amanda|date=2012-12-20|title=Role Models and the Psychological Characteristics That Buffer Low-Socioeconomic-Status Youth From Cardiovascular Risk|journal=Child Development|volume=84|issue=4|pages=1241–1252|doi=10.1111/cdev.12037|pmid=23278857|issn=0009-3920|url=https://semanticscholar.org/paper/88d49304c17eba067f622a155ea7032e7b8c05d5}}</ref>
* A “healthier” profile of [[hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis]] functioning, as indexed by diurnal [[cortisol]] in children from low [[socioeconomic status]] families.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Chen|first=Lihua|last2=Li|first2=Xiaoming|last3=Imami|first3=Ledina|last4=Lin|first4=Danhua|last5=Zhao|first5=Junfeng|last6=Zhao|first6=Guoxiang|last7=Zilioli|first7=Samuele|date=2019|title=Diurnal Cortisol in a Sample of Socioeconomically Disadvantaged Chinese Children: Evidence for the Shift-and-Persist Hypothesis|journal=Psychosomatic Medicine|volume=81|issue=2|pages=200–208|doi=10.1097/PSY.0000000000000659|issn=1534-7796|pmc=6355348|pmid=30531205}}</ref>
* Lower levels of [[asthma]]-related impairment and [[inflammation]] in children from low, but not high, [[socioeconomic status]] families.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Chen|first=Edith|last2=Strunk|first2=Robert C.|last3=Trethewey|first3=Alexandra|last4=Schreier|first4=Hannah M. C.|last5=Maharaj|first5=Nandini|last6=Miller|first6=Gregory E.|date=2011|title=Resilience in low-socioeconomic-status children with asthma: adaptations to stress|journal=The Journal of Allergy and Clinical Immunology|volume=128|issue=5|pages=970–976|doi=10.1016/j.jaci.2011.06.040|issn=1097-6825|pmc=3205307|pmid=21824649}}</ref>
* Better [[asthma]] profiles in children and teens from families reporting low, but not high, perceived social status.<ref name=":4">{{Cite journal|last=Lam|first=Phoebe H.|last2=Miller|first2=Gregory E.|last3=Chiang|first3=Jessica J.|last4=Levine|first4=Cynthia S.|last5=Le|first5=Van|last6=Shalowitz|first6=Madeleine U.|last7=Story|first7=Rachel E.|last8=Chen|first8=Edith|date=2018-08-06|title=One size does not fit all: Links between shift-and-persist and asthma in youth are moderated by perceived social status and experience of unfair treatment|journal=Development and Psychopathology|volume=30|issue=5|pages=1699–1714|doi=10.1017/s0954579418000913|pmid=30078386|issn=0954-5794|doi-access=free}}</ref>
* Lower levels of [[Depression (mood)|depressive symptoms]] in [[Latinx]] youth from low, but not high, income families.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Christophe|first=N. Keita|last2=Stein|first2=Gabriela Livas|last3=Martin Romero|first3=Michelle Y.|last4=Chan|first4=Michele|last5=Jensen|first5=Michaeline|last6=Gonzalez|first6=Laura M.|last7=Kiang|first7=Lisa|date=2019-08-01|title=Coping and Culture: The Protective Effects of Shift-&-Persist and Ethnic-Racial Identity on Depressive Symptoms in Latinx Youth|journal=Journal of Youth and Adolescence|language=en|volume=48|issue=8|pages=1592–1604|doi=10.1007/s10964-019-01037-8|pmid=31134561|issn=1573-6601}}</ref>

Although it has been proposed that a variety of psychological interventions for at-risk youth of low [[socioeconomic status]] may reduce [[health disparities]], in part, by increasing shift-and-persist tendencies in families, the majority of studies on shift-and-persist have been cross-sectional. Therefore, it remains unknown if shift-and-persist strategies play a [[Causality|causal role]] in reducing the negative impact of low [[socioeconomic status]] on health.<ref name=":4" /><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Miller|first=Gregory E.|last2=Brody|first2=Gene H.|last3=Yu|first3=Tianyi|last4=Chen|first4=Edith|date=2014-08-05|title=A family-oriented psychosocial intervention reduces inflammation in low-SES African American youth|journal=Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences|language=en|volume=111|issue=31|pages=11287–11292|doi=10.1073/pnas.1406578111|issn=0027-8424|pmid=25049403|pmc=4128159|bibcode=2014PNAS..11111287M}}</ref> More longitudinal and treatment studies are needed to evaluate directional and causal hypotheses based upon the shift-and-persist model.
