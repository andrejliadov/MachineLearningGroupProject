'''Bawit''' ({{lang-ar|باويط}} ''Bāwīṭ''; [[Coptic language|Coptic:]] ⲡⲁⲩⲏⲧ ''Bawet''<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.trismegistos.org/place/10454|title=Trismegistos|website=www.trismegistos.org|access-date=2017-03-05}}</ref>) is an [[archaeology|archaeological]] site located {{convert|80|km}} north of [[Asyut]], near the village of Dashlout, in Egypt. It covers an area of {{convert|40|ha}}, and houses a cemetery and the ruins of the Hermopolite Monastery of [[Apa Apollo]] founded by [[Abib and Apollo|Apollo]] in the late fourth century. The structures on this site are relatively well preserved, and demonstrate different aspects of a monastic complex of [[Middle Egypt]].

==History==
The Apa Apolla monastery ([[Coptic language|Coptic]]: ⲧⲟⲡⲟⲥ ⲛⲁⲡⲁ ⲁⲡⲟⲗⲗⲱ) is a Coptic monastery founded c. 385/390 and had about 500 monks.<ref name="Badawy1978">{{cite book|last=Badawy|first=Alexander|title=Coptic art and archaeology: the art of the Christian Egyptians from the late antique to the Middle Ages|url=https://archive.org/details/copticartarchaeo00alex|url-access=registration|accessdate=26 March 2012|year=1978|publisher=MIT Press|page=500}}</ref> The sixth and seventh centuries were a period of prosperity for this monastery, which then hosted a community of women, under the patronage of [[St. Rachel]].<ref name="MikhailMoussa2009">{{cite book|last1=Mikhail|first1=Magad S. A.|last2=Moussa|first2=Mark|title=Christianity and monasticism in Wadi al-Natrun: essays from the 2002 international symposium of the Saint Mark Foundation and the Saint Shenouda the Archimandrite Coptic Society|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=4eBv2vuo7lYC&pg=PA226|accessdate=26 March 2012|date=30 April 2009|publisher=American Univ in Cairo Press|isbn=978-977-416-260-2|page=226}}</ref> A fresco found at the monastery depicting St. Rachel dates to the sixth century.<ref name="MelvilleMüller2012">{{cite book|last1=Melville|first1=Gert|last2=Müller|first2=Anne|title=Female 'vita Religiosa' Between Late Antiquity and the High Middle Ages: Structures, Developments and Spatial Contexts|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=HGq7_HWU8nEC&pg=PA41|accessdate=26 March 2012|date=January 2012|publisher=LIT Verlag Münster|isbn=978-3-643-90124-8|page=41}}</ref> After the Islamic invasion, the monastery declined, and was abandoned around the tenth century.

==Excavation==
In early 1901, a survey of the site and <big>surrounding</big> areas was made by [[Jean Clédat]], who was based at the [[Institut Français d'Archéologie Orientale|French Institute of Oriental Archaeology]] in Cairo. Continuing into 1902, Clédat was assisted by [[Émile Gaston Chassinat]] and [[Charles Palanque]].<ref name="LibraryGottheil1925">{{cite book|author1=New York Public Library|author2=Richard James Horatio Gottheil|title=Ancient Egypt: sources of information in the New York public library|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=pkC2AAAAIAAJ|accessdate=26 March 2012|year=1925|publisher=The New York public library|page=5}}</ref> Clédat found [[Hermitage (religious retreat)|hermitages]] he called "chapels" that contained [[Coptic art]]. His colleagues discovered two churches, today simply called North and South Church, with stone and wood carvings that were removed to the [[Coptic Museum]] in Cairo and the [[Musée du Louvre]] in [[Paris]]. Numerous sculptures and paintings were unearthed during the excavations.<ref name="GabraVivian2002">{{cite book|last1=Gabra|first1=Gawdat|last2=Vivian|first2=Tim|title=Coptic Monasteries: Egypt's Monastic Art and Architecture|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=csmfAAAAMAAJ|accessdate=26 March 2012|year=2002|publisher=American University in Cairo Press|isbn=978-977-424-691-3|pages=116–18}}</ref> The papyrologist [[Jean Maspero]] (1885–1915)  resumed excavations in 1913, discovering a common room with several entrances.  In 1976, then 1984 and 1985, the [[Supreme Council of Antiquities]] resumed excavations and added to the collections of the Coptic Museum. Since then, excavations have continued under various organizations.
