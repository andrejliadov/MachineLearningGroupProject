'''Multiconfessional''' countries have a power sharing arrangement between people of different faiths, usually three or more significant confessional groups within the same jurisdiction. Examples of modern countries deemed multiconfessional are [[Religion in Lebanon|Lebanon]]{{sfn|Dawahare|1998}}<ref>{{cite book|author1=Sjur Bergan|author2=Hilligje van't Land|title=Speaking Across Borders: The Role of Higher Education in Furthering Intercultural Dialogue|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=cSq7GN3ZwGQC&pg=PA167|year=2010|publisher=Council of Europe|isbn=978-92-871-6941-9|pages=167â€“}}</ref> and [[Religion in Bosnia and Herzegovina|Bosnia and Herzegovina]].<ref>{{cite book|author1=Mary McIntosh|author2=Dan Abele|author3=University of Strathclyde. Centre for the Study of Public Policy|title=Tolerance for a multiethnic Bosnia-Hercegovina: testing alternative theories|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=V6QtAQAAIAAJ|year=1996|publisher=Centre for the Study of Public Policy, University of Strathclyde}}</ref> 

The "[[National Pact]]" in [[Lebanon]] is a formal agreement altering the 1926 Constitution, which laid the foundation of Lebanon as a [[Confessionalism (politics)|confessionalist]] state. Instead of a minority wielding the most power, political power became more representative.<ref>{{cite book|author=R. Rabil|title=Religion, National Identity, and Confessional Politics in Lebanon: The Challenge of Islamism|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=3fHMCwAAQBAJ|date=12 September 2011|publisher=Palgrave Macmillan US|isbn=978-0-230-33925-5}}</ref>
