'''Roderick Aldon Walcott''', [[Officer of the Order of the British Empire|OBE]] (23 January 1930{{spaced ndash}}6 March 2000), was a [[St Lucia]]n playwright, screenwriter, painter, theatre director, costume and set designer, lyricist and literary editor.<ref name=Voice>[http://www.thevoiceslu.com/features/2011/november/19_11_11/Roderick.htm "Roderick Walcott – A Brief Biography"], ''The Voice Online'', 19 November 2011.</ref> As a dramatist he "has been recognised as one of the most committed figures in the effort to develop a distinctive Caribbean theatre in the region".<ref name=Hughes>Michael Hughes, ''A Companion to West Indian Literature'', Collins, 1979, pp. 130–131.{{isbn|978-0003252804}}</ref><!--A square in Castries,St.Lucia is named after his brother,Derek Walcott.The name of the square is, "The Derek Walcott Square." irrelevant here!--> He was the twin brother of Nobel laureate [[Derek Walcott]].

==Life and career==
Roderick Aldon Walcott was born in [[Castries]], St Lucia, the son of Alix (Maarlin) and Warwick Walcott.<ref>[[Hilton Als]], [http://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2004/02/09/the-islander "The Islander"], ''[[The New Yorker]]'', 9 February 2004.</ref> He was educated at [[Saint Mary's College (Saint Lucia)|St Mary's College]] there. In 1950, he (together with his brother Derek and friends) was instrumental in founding the St Lucia Arts Guild, to read and perform plays.<ref name=Hughes /> He wrote, produced and directed plays with the Arts Guild during the 1950s and 1960s, and is regarded as "one of the founders of modern Caribbean theatre",<ref name=Voice /> building and fostering a local homegrown audience.

In 1968, he moved to [[Canada]] where he studied Theatre Arts at [[York University]] in [[Toronto]] from 1969 to 1973. He returned temporarily to St Lucia in 1977 to become the first Director of Culture (1977–80).{{cn|date=June 2018}}

He was the author of several plays, many of them published by the Extra-Mural Department of the [[University of the West Indies]]. His play ''The Harrowing of Benjy'' is the most produced play in the English-speaking Caribbean.<ref name=Voice /> He also wrote numerous musicals,<ref>Jacques Compton, [http://news.stluciastar.com/is-the-government-about-to-insult-the-art-world/ "The legacy of the Walcott Brothers"], ''St Lucia Star'', 16 September 2010.</ref> of which ''The Banjo Man'', a collaboration with the composer Charles Cadet, was successfully staged at [[Carifesta]] 1972 in [[Guyana]] and throughout the greater Caribbean. Walcott is also acknowledged as a pioneer of Carnival in St Lucia.<ref name=Voice />

Roderick Walcott died at his home in Toronto, Canada, in 2000 at the age of 70, after a long illness.<ref>[http://www.slucia.com/archive/news/old_news_250300.html#2 "Roddy Walcott (70) passes away"], ''St Lucia Online: Last Week's News'', 11 March 2000.</ref> His death is a theme in his brother Derek's 2004 work ''The Prodigal''.<ref>[[Mary Jo Salter]], [https://www.nytimes.com/2004/10/31/books/review/31SALTERL.html "'The Prodigal': The Wanderer"], ''[[The New York Times]]'', 31 October 2004.</ref><ref>[[Edward Baugh]], [http://caribbean-beat.com/online-exclusives/homecoming "Homecoming"], ''Caribbean Beat''. From ''The Caribbean Review of Books'', August 2004.</ref>

In 2009, a collection of Roderick Walcott's works was donated to the University of the West Indies Open Campus in St Lucia.<ref>[http://www.thevoiceslu.com/let_and_op/2009/april/16_04_09/Roderick_Walcott_Collection_Finds_a_New_Home.htm "Walcott Collection Finds a New Home"], ''The Voice'' (St Lucia), 16 April 2009.</ref>
