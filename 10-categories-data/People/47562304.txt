'''Mohammad Yousuf Abul Farah Tartusi''' ({{lang-ar|محمد یوسف ابوالفرح طرطوسی}}) was a popular [[Sufi Islam|Sufi Muslim]] [[List of Sufi saints|saint]]. He is regarded as one of the common ancestors of the Sufi [[Tariqah]]s, which form an unbroken [[silsilah|chain]] to the [[Islamic prophet]] [[Mohammad]].<ref>Historical dictionary of Sufism By John Renard</ref><ref name="The Qadiriyya Order of Sufism">{{cite book|author=Sāiyid Athar Abbas Rizvi|title=A History of Sufism in India: From sixteenth century to modern century Volume 2 |url=https://books.google.com/?id=4FzYAAAAMAAJ&q=abu+al+farah+tartusi&dq=abu+al+farah+tartusi|publisher=Munshiram Manoharlal |year= 1992}}</ref>

==Biography==
Mohammad Yousuf Abul Farah Tartusi born on August 21, 1016 CE (15 Rabi ul Awwal 407 AH) in Tartus, Syria. His father's name was Shaikh Abdullah bin Younus Tartusi.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://muqtadaria.com/dqcnm/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=193&Itemid=189|title=Hazrat Abul Farah Tartoosi - Biography|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160421035942/http://muqtadaria.com/dqcnm/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=193&Itemid=189|archive-date=2016-04-21}}</ref> His given name was Mohammad Yousuf, while his [[patronymic]] was Abul Farah. He is sometimes given the title Alauddin.

He was known as a [[Qutb]], which in Sufism is a perfect human being, otherwise known as ''[[al-insān al-kāmil]]'', "The Universal Man" at  the top of the saintly hierarchy.<ref name="Insan e Kamil (The Universal Man)">{{cite book|author=Sult̤ān Mohammad Najib-ur-Rehman|title=Sultan Bahoo: The Life and Teachings |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=hlyMAwAAQBAJ&pg=PA292|publisher=Sultan-ul-Faqr Publications|isbn= 978-9-699-79518-3|date=2015-03-11 }}</ref>{{unreliable source?|date=July 2018}} of his time known for performing [[Karamat|miracles]].<ref name="KaramustafaAhmet">Karamustafa, Ahmet T.. Sufism: The Formative Period. Edinburgh, UK: Edinburgh University Press, 2007. Print.</ref> He was said to have had such intense level of ''[[tawakkul]]'' and ''[[sabr]]'' that [[Detachment (philosophy)|worldly matters did not concern him]].<ref name="Hamdy 2009 173–196">{{cite journal|last=Hamdy|first=Sherine|title=Islam, Fatalism, and Medical Intervention: Lessons from Egypt on the Cultivation of Forbearance (Sabr) and Reliance of God (Tawakkul)|journal=Anthropological Quarterly|year=2009|volume=82|issue=1|pages=173–196|doi=10.1353/anq.0.0053|url=http://www.escholarship.org/uc/item/8bx5098r|access-date=2020-07-02|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20181027101444/https://escholarship.org/uc/item/8bx5098r|archive-date=2018-10-27|url-status=live}}</ref>

His [[khalifa|spiritual successor]] was [[Abul Hasan Hankari|Shaikh Abul Hassan Ali bin Mohammad Qureshi Hakkari]].{{citation  needed|date=July 2018}}

Abul Farah Tartusi died on October 28, 1055 [[Common Era|CE]] (3 [[Sha'aban]] 447 [[Islamic Calendar|AH]]), during the [[Abbasid Caliphate]]. His [[Mazar (mausoleum)|mausoleum]] is in [[Baghdad]], [[Iraq]].{{citation  needed|date=July 2018}}
