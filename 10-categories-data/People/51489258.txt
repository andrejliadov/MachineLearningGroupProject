'''Johanna Catharina Cornelius''' (February 27, 1912 – June 21, 1974) was an [[Afrikaners|Afrikaner]] activist and [[trade union]]ist. She served as the Afrikaner [[Textile and clothing trade unions|Garment Worker's Union]] (GWU) president after [[Solly Sachs]].

== Biography ==
Cornelius was born in [[Lichtenburg, North West|Lichtenburg, South Africa]], and grew up in rural [[South Africa]] as one of nine children.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|url=https://archive.org/details/encyclopediaofwo0000rapp|url-access=registration|quote=johanna cornelius.|title=Encyclopedia of Women Social Reformers|last=Rappaport|first=Helen|publisher=ABC-CLIO|year=2001|isbn=1576071014|location=|pages=[https://archive.org/details/encyclopediaofwo0000rapp/page/164 164]–165|via=}}</ref> Her father and grandfather both fought in the [[Second Boer War|Anglo Boer War]] and her mother was held in a [[Internment|concentration camp]] during the war.<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|url=http://www.sahistory.org.za/people/johanna-catharina-cornelius|title=Johanna Catharina Cornelius|last=|first=|date=28 November 2011|website=South African History Online|publisher=|access-date=1 September 2016}}</ref> She and her older sister, Hester Cornelius, moved to [[Johannesburg]] in the 1920s, where Johanna eventually started working in a garment factory.<ref name=":0" /> Cornelius worked as a machinist in the factory.<ref name=":2" />

Cornelius was arrested and detained in jail for several house in 1932 while participating in a GWU strike.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=http://www.sahistory.org.za/article/rise-women%E2%80%99s-trade-unionism-south-africa-peter-lanasa|title=The Rise of Women's Trade Unionism in South Africa|last=LaNasa|first=Peter|date=12 June 2015|website=South African History Online|publisher=|access-date=1 September 2016}}</ref> After she was released from jail, she spoke to the workers and encouraged them to "demand a living wage and freedom."<ref name=":1" /> Her speech also referenced the [[Great Trek]] and the Anglo Boer War, joining "nationalism together with the class struggle rather than with the national struggle."<ref name=":2" /> She went to the [[Soviet Union]] as part of a workers' delegation in 1933.<ref name=":0" /> Her trip there helped her learn more about [[communism]] and a sense of social equality.<ref name=":1" /> When she returned from the Soviet Union, she became a full-time union organizer for GWU, working from the main office in [[Germiston]].<ref name=":0" />

Cornelius worked as the GWU president from 1935 to 1937 and under her leadership, the union won reduced working hours and increased wages for workers.<ref name=":0" /> Cornelius worked to include people of all backgrounds in the union and felt that working in the union had helped her "transcend the racial attitudes" of her past.{{Sfn|Berger|1992|p=124}} She and Hester also traveled to [[Cape Town]] in February 1936 help the GWU branch there.<ref name=":2" />

Cornelius was accused in 1938 of being a "communist accomplice of Sachs and for spending all her time organising black people."<ref name=":2" /> However, she was able to successfully fight off attempts by [[Afrikaner nationalism|Afrikaner nationalists]] who wanted to take control of the union.<ref name=":0" /> She became a founder of the National Union of Cigarette and Tobacco Workers in 1938, later leading a two-week strike in [[Rustenburg]] in September 1940.<ref name=":0" /> The strike in Rustenberg was "heated" and women in the strike faced [[tear gas]] and police attacks.{{Sfn|Berger|1992|p=158}}

In 1943 she ran unsuccessfully as an Independent Labor Party candidate.<ref name=":0" /> When Solly Sachs was exiled from South Africa in 1952, she took over the GWU and worked that position until her death.<ref name=":0" />

Cornelius died on June 21, 1974, in Johannesburg.<ref name=":2" />
