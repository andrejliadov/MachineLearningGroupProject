'''Robert Woodruff Anderson''' (April 28, 1917 – February 9, 2009)<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.findagrave.com/memorial/33706453/robert-woodruff-anderson|title=Robert Woodruff Anderson (1917–2009)|website=[[Find a Grave]]|accessdate=June 1, 2018}}</ref> was an [[Americans|American]] playwright, screenwriter, and theatrical producer. He received two [[Academy Awards|Academy Award]] nominations for [[Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay|Best Writing, Screenplay Based on Material from Another Medium]], for the drama films ''[[The Nun's Story (film)|The Nun's Story]]'' (1959) and ''[[I Never Sang for My Father]]'' (1970).<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1988/06/12/theater/robert-anderson-the-drama-of-being-a-dramatist.html|newspaper=New York Times|date=12 June 1988|title=Robert Anderson: The Drama of Being a Dramatist|first=Herbert|last=Mitgang}}</ref>

==Life and career==
Anderson was born in New York City, the son of Myra Esther (Grigg) and James Hewston Anderson, a self-made businessman.<ref>https://www.enotes.com/topics/robert-anderson</ref> He was educated at [[Phillips Exeter Academy]], which he later said he found a lonely experience. While there he fell in love with an older woman, an event which later became the basis of the plot of ''[[Tea and Sympathy (play)|Tea and Sympathy]]''. Anderson also attended [[Harvard University]], where he took an undergraduate as well as a master's degree.<ref>[https://www.nytimes.com/2009/02/10/theater/10anderson.html "Robert Anderson, Playwright of Tea and Sympathy, Dies at 91," ''The New York Times'', February 10, 2009]</ref>

He may be best-remembered as the author of ''[[Tea and Sympathy (play)|Tea and Sympathy]]''. The play made its [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]] debut in 1953 and was made into a [[Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer]] film in 1956; both starred [[Deborah Kerr]] and [[John Kerr (actor)|John Kerr]].

''[[You Know I Can't Hear You When the Water's Running]]'', a collection of four one-act comedies, opened in New York in 1967 and ran for more than 700 performances. His other successful [[Broadway theatre|Broadway]] plays were ''Silent Night, Lonely Night'' (1959) and ''[[I Never Sang for My Father]]'' (1968).<ref>[https://www.google.com/hostednews/ap/article/ALeqM5husmUxkUfxSYCLQNjXauPKRjglrgD968EMPO0 Michael Kuchwara, "Robert Anderson, 'Tea and Sympathy' author, dies," AP, February 9, 2009]</ref>

He wrote the screenplays for ''[[Until They Sail]]'' (1957), ''[[The Nun's Story (film)|The Nun's Story]]'' (1959), and ''[[The Sand Pebbles (film)|The Sand Pebbles]]'' (1966). He also authored many television scripts, including the TV play ''The Last Act Is a Solo'' (1991), and the novels ''After'' (1973) and ''Getting Up and Going Home'' (1978).

He was inducted into the [[American Theater Hall of Fame]] in 1981.<ref>[https://www.nytimes.com/1981/03/03/theater/26-elected-theater-hall-fame-26-broadway-voted-into-theater-hall-fame.html ''The New York Times'', March 3, 1981 - ''26 Elected to the Theater Hall of Fame'']</ref>

Anderson was married to Phyllis Stohl from 1940 until her death in 1956 and to actress [[Teresa Wright]] from 1959 until their divorce in 1978. Anderson died of [[pneumonia]] on February 9, 2009 at his home in [[Manhattan]], aged 91. He had been suffering from [[Alzheimer's disease]] for seven years prior to his death.<ref>{{Cite web |url=http://www.playbill.com/news/article/126128.html# |title=Biodata in ''Playbill'' magazine |access-date=2009-02-10 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090213103323/http://playbill.com/news/article/126128.html# |archive-date=2009-02-13 |url-status=dead }}</ref>

==Advocacy==
As a supporter for writers' rights in theatre, Anderson was a member of the [[Dramatists Guild of America]] and was elected president in 1971. He continued to serve the non-profit organization until 1973.
