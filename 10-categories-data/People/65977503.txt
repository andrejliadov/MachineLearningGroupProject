'''Frances Burgess''' ({{nee|'''Lockett'''}}; 1910–1993) was an English woman and the first person to be crowned the national [[Queens of Industry|Cotton Queen]] in 1930.  She held the position for one year, travelling the country to promote business and meeting high-ranking politicians.  After her term, she returned to her job as a weaver in a cotton mill.

== Biography ==
Burgess was born Frances Lockett in 1910 in [[Hyde, Greater Manchester]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|last=Plaques|first=Open|title=Frances Lockett blue plaque|url=https://openplaques.org/plaques/8026|access-date=30 November 2020|website=openplaques.org|language=en-GB}}</ref> Her father was a policeman.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|last=Cornes|first=Rachel|last2=Post|first2=18-01-18|title=The story of Frances Lockett, Britain’s first Cotton Queen|url=https://museumcrush.org/the-story-of-frances-lockett-britains-first-cotton-queen/|access-date=30 November 2020|website=Museum Crush|language=en-GB}}</ref> She left school aged 14 and started work as a weaver at Newton Mill in Hyde.<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=Frances Burgess (née Lockett) (1910-1993)|url=https://www.tameside.gov.uk/TouristInformationCentre/Blue-Plaque-Frances-Burgess-%28nee-Lockett%29|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201218134000/https://www.tameside.gov.uk/TouristInformationCentre/Blue-Plaque-Frances-Burgess-%28nee-Lockett%29|archive-date=18 December 2020|access-date=|website=Tameside Council}}</ref> In 1930 she was selected to be Hyde's first [[Queens of Industry|Cotton Queen]]; later the same year she became the first national Cotton Queen and was crowned in [[Blackpool]].<ref name=":2" /><ref>{{Cite web|title=Photograph of Frances Lockett|url=https://artsandculture.google.com/asset/photograph-of-frances-lockett/gQE6iRMcLYAMnw|access-date=30 November 2020|website=Google Arts & Culture|language=en}}</ref> On her return to Hyde, after being crowned, she was greeted by a crowd of 20,000 people.<ref name=":2" />  The town was decorated for this procession and the parade included Lockett in an open carriage, mounted police, Morris dancers, the Kingston Mill Band, Hyde Borough Brass Band, and over 300 workers from Newton Mill.<ref name=":2" />

In the year after she was crowned, Lockett undertook a tour attending events to boost the reputation of the cotton industry. As part of this she met politicians [[Stanley Baldwin]], [[Ramsay MacDonald]], [[David Lloyd George|Lloyd George]] and [[Margaret Bondfield]].<ref name=":2" /><ref name=":4">{{Cite web|title=New exhibition in Leeds will honour Yorkshire's queens of the industrial age|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201218133728/https://www.yorkshireeveningpost.co.uk/news/new-exhibition-leeds-will-honour-yorkshires-queens-industrial-age-1779596|access-date=30 November 2020|website=www.yorkshireeveningpost.co.uk|language=en}}</ref> Lloyd George reportedly touched her dress and inquired where he could buy some of the same material it was made from for his daughter.<ref>{{Cite web|date=3 November 2017|title=Coalpit pin-ups: Leeds museum pays tribute to queens of industrial age|url=http://www.theguardian.com/culture/2017/nov/03/coalpit-pin-ups-leeds-museum-tribute-queens-industrial-age|access-date=30 November 2020|website=the Guardian|language=en}}</ref> She also travelled the country visiting department stores to<ref name=":4" /> encourage sales of cotton, often chaperoned by her employer's wife.<ref name=":3">{{Cite journal|last=Conway|first=R.|date=1 December 2013|title=Making the Mill Girl Modern?: Beauty, Industry, and the Popular Newspaper in 1930s' England|url=https://academic.oup.com/tcbh/article-lookup/doi/10.1093/tcbh/hwt004|journal=Twentieth Century British History|language=en|volume=24|issue=4|pages=518–541|doi=10.1093/tcbh/hwt004|issn=0955-2359}}</ref> Lockett recalled in later life that she knew Cotton Queen "wasn’t going to be just like a Rose Queen; you were going to be kind of an ambassador for cotton".<ref name=":3" /> 

After her year as Cotton Queen finished, Lockett returned to Hyde and continued to work as a weaver at J & J Ashton's Mill.<ref name=":2" /> In 1937 she married policeman, James Burgess.<ref name=":2" /> They had no children.<ref name=":1" />

Lockett died in 1993.<ref name=":0" /> She was a local celebrity until her death.<ref>{{Cite web|last=Barlow|first=Nigel|date=9 February 2014|title=Cotton Queen|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20201218133603/https://aboutmanchester.co.uk/cotton-queen/|access-date=30 November 2020|website=About Manchester|language=en-GB}}</ref>

== Legacy ==
[[File:Frances_Burgess.jpg|thumb|Frances Burgess (née Lockett), Cotton Queen]]
Lockett's success as Cotton Queen was commemorated in the popular song "Waltz of the Cotton Queen".<ref name=":2" /> There was also merchandise produced to mark her time as queen, including postcards and handkerchiefs made from "Queenetta" fabric.<ref name=":1" />

On 13 March 2007, a [[blue plaque]] was unveiled in Hyde, dedicated to her life.<ref name=":0" /> Lockett's story was one of several to feature in an exhibition at [[Leeds Industrial Museum at Armley Mills|Leeds Industrial Museum]] in 2017.
