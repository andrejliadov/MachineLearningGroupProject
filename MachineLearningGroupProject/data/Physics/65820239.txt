'''Tau functions''' are an important ingredient in the modern theory of [[integrable system]]s, and have numerous applications in a variety of other domains. Before being named as such, they were effectively introduced by ''Ryogo Hirota'' <ref> Hirota, Ryogo, Ryogo Hirota, "Reduction of soliton equations in bilinear form",
''Physica D, Nonlinear Phenomena'' '''18''' , 161-170 (1986)</ref> in his approach to integrable systems, based on expressing them in an equivalent bilinear form. The term ''Tau function'', or ''<math> \tau </math>-function'', seems to have first been used systematically by [[Mikio Sato|Sato]]<ref name="Sato">M. Sato,  "Soliton equations as dynamical systems on infinite dimensional Grassmann manifolds", ''Kokyuroku, RIMS, Kyoto Univ.'', 30–46 (1981).</ref> and his students <ref> E. Date, M. Jimbo, M. Kashiwara and T. Miwa,  "Operator approach to the Kadomtsev-Petviashvili equation III". ''J. Phys. Soc. Jap.''  '''50''' (11): 3806–3812 (1981). doi:10.1143/JPSJ.50.3806.</ref> <ref>M. Jimbo and T. Miwa,  "Solitons and infinite-dimensional Lie algebras", ''Publ. Res. Inst. Math. Sci.'', '''19'''(3):943–1001 (1983).</ref> in the specific context of the integrable KP or [[Kadomtsev–Petviashvili equation |Kadomtsev–Petviashvili equation]], and related integrable hierarchies. It is a central ingredient in the theory of [[solitons]]. Tau functions also appear in the guise of matrix model partition functions in the spectral theory of [[Random matrix|Random Matrices]]. They also appear as [[generating function]]s, in the sense of combinatorics and [[enumerative geometry]], especially in relation to [[moduli spaces]] of Riemann surfaces, and enumeration of branched coverings, or so-called [[ELSV formula|Hurwitz numbers]].

==Definition of <math>\tau</math>-functions==
There are two notions of <math>\tau </math>-functions, both introduced by the [[Mikio Sato |Sato]] school. The first is the notion of ''[[Isomonodromic deformation |isomonodromic]] <math>\tau </math>-function'' <ref name="JMU"/>. The second is the ''<math>\tau </math>-function of the [[Mikio Sato |Sato]]-[[Graeme Segal|Segal]]-Wilson type'' <ref name="Sato"/><ref name="SW"/>, for integrable hierarchies satisfying [[isospectral]] deformation equations of [[Peter Lax | Lax]] type.
 
A <math> \tau </math>-function of the ''isospectral'' type is a solution of the Hirota bilinear equations. Geometrically, in  the Sato<ref name="Sato"/> and Segal-Wilson <ref name="SW"> G. Segal, G, Wilson, G., "Loop groups and equations of KdV type", ''Inst. Hautes Etudes Sci. Publ. Math.'', '''6''' (61), 5–65 (1985)</ref> sense, it is the value of the determinant of a [[Fredholm integral operator]], interpreted as the orthogonal projection of an element of a suitably defined (infinite dimensional) Grassmann manifold onto the ''origin'', as that element evolves under the linear exponential action of a maximal abelian subgroup of the general linear group. It typically arises as a [[Partition function (statistical mechanics)|partition function]], in the sense of [[statistical mechanics]], many-body [[quantum mechanics]] or [[quantum field theory]], as the underlying measure undergoes a linear exponential deformation.

==Hirota bilinear residue equation for KP <math>\tau</math>-functions==

A KP ([[Kadomtsev–Petviashvili equation |Kadomtsev–Petviashvili]]) <math>\tau</math>-function  <math>\tau(\mathbf{t})</math> 
is a function of an infinite number of KP flow variables <math>\mathbf{t}=(t_1, t_2, \dots)</math> that satisfies the
following bilinear formal residue equation
{{NumBlk|::| <math> \mathrm{res}_{z=0}\left(e^{\sum_{i=1}^\infty (\delta t_i)z^i} \tau({\bf t} - [z^{-1}])\tau({\bf s} + [z^{-1}])\right)dz  \equiv 0, </math>|{{EquationRef|1}}}}
identically in the <math> \delta t_j </math> variables, where <math>\mathrm{res}_{z=0}</math> is the 
<math>z^{-1}</math> coefficient in the formal Laurent expansion resulting from expanding all factors as Laurent series' in
<math>z</math>, and

:: <math> {\bf s} := {\bf t} + (\delta t_1, \delta t_2, \cdots  ), \quad [z^{-1}] := (z^{-1}, \tfrac{z^{-2}}{2}, \cdots \tfrac{z^{-j}}{j}, \cdots).  </math>

==Kadomtsev-Petviashvili equation==

If  <math>\tau(t_1, t_2, t_3, \dots\dots) </math> is a KP <math>\tau </math>  function satisfying  
the Hirota residue equation ({{EquationNote|1}})  and we identify the first three flow variables as
::<math>t_1 =x,  t_2=y, t_3 =t, </math>
it follows that the function
::<math>u(x,y,t):=2\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\log\left(\tau(x,y,t, t_4,\dots)\right) </math>
satisfies the <math>2+1</math> dimensional nonlinear partial differential equation
::<math>3u_{yy}=\left(4u_t-6uu_x-u_{xxx}\right)_x, </math>
known as the [[Kadomtsev–Petviashvili equation|''Kadomtsev-Petviashvili'' (KP)  ''equation'']], which plays a prominent role in plasma physics
and in shallow water ocean waves. 

Taking further logarithmic derivatives of <math>\tau(t_1, t_2, t_3, \dots\dots)</math> gives an infinite sequence of functions 
that  satisfy further systems of nonlinear autonomous PDE's, each involving partial derivatives of finite order 
with respect to a finite number of the KP flow parameters <math>{\bf t} =(t_1, t_2, \dots )</math>. 
These are collectively known as the ''KP hierarchy''.

==Fuchsian isomonodromic systems: Schlesinger equations. Isomonodromic <math> \tau </math>-functions==

Consider the [[overdetermined system]] of first order matrix partial differential equations
: <math>{\partial \Psi \over \partial z}- \sum_{i=1}^n {N_i \over z - \alpha_i} \Psi=0, \quad (*) </math>
: <math> {\partial \Psi \over \partial \alpha_i}+ {N_i \over z - \alpha_i} \Psi=0 \quad (*) </math>
where <math> \{N_i\}_{i=1, \dots , n}</math> are a set of <math>n</math> <math>r\times r</math> traceless matrices, 
<math>\{\alpha_i\}_{i=1, \dots , n}</math> a set of <math>n</math> complex parameters and <math>z</math> a complex variable, and <math>\Psi(z, \alpha_1, \dots , \alpha_m)</math> is an invertible <math>n \times n</math> matrix valued function of <math>z</math> and <math>\{\alpha_i\}_{i=1, \dots , n}</math>.
These are the necessary and sufficient conditions for the based [[monodromy]] representation of the fundamental group
<math>\pi_0({\bf P}^1\backslash\{\alpha_i\}_{i=1, \dots , n})</math> of the Riemann sphere punctured at
the points <math>\{\alpha_i\}_{i=1, \dots , n}</math>   correspoding to the rational covariant derivative operator 
<math>{\partial  \over \partial z}- \sum_{i=1}^n {N_i \over z - \alpha_i}</math> to
be independent of the parameters <math>\{\alpha_i\}_{i=1, \dots , n}</math>; i.e. that changes in these parameters induce an [[isomonodromic deformation]]. The compatibility conditions for this system are the [[Schlesinger equations]]:<ref name="JMU"> M. Jimbo, T.  Miwa,  and K.  Ueno, "Monodromy Preserving Deformation of Linear Ordinary Differential Equations with Rational Coefficients I", ''Physica D,''  '''2''', 306–352 (1981). </ref>
:<math> {\partial N_i \over \partial \alpha_j} = {[N_i, N_j] \over \alpha_i-\alpha_j} \quad \text{ for } i \neq j,</math>
:<math>{\partial N_i \over \partial \alpha_i} = - \sum_{1\le j \le n, j\neq i}{[N_i, N_j] \over \alpha_i-\alpha_j}. </math> 

Defining the <math>n</math> functions
:<math> H_i = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{1\le j \le n, j\neq i}{{\rm Tr}(N_i N_j) \over \alpha_i-\alpha_j}, \quad i=1, \dots ,n, </math> 
the Schlesinger equations imply that the differential form
:<math>\omega := \sum_{i=1}^n H_i d\alpha_i</math>
on the space of parameters is closed:
:<math>d\omega = 0</math>
and hence, locally exact. Therefore, at least locally, there exists a function <math>\tau(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_n)</math>
of the parameters, defined within a multiplicative constant, such that
:<math>\omega = d\mathrm{ln}\tau </math>
The function <math>\tau(\alpha_1, \dots, \alpha_n)</math> is called the ''isomonodromic <math>\tau</math>-function''
associated to the fundamental solution <math>\Psi</math> of the system <math>(*)</math>.
For non-Fuchsian systems, with higher order poles, the ''generalized'' monodromy data includes ''[[Stokes parameters]]'' and ''connection matrices'', and there are further [[isomonodromic deformation]] parameters associated with the local asymptotics, but the ''isomonodromic <math> \tau </math>-functions'' may be defined in a similar way, using differentials on the extended parameter space.<ref name="JMU"/>
