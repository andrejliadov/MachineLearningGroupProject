[[Albert Einstein]] (1879–1955) was a renowned [[theoretical physics|theoretical physicist]] of the 20th century, best known for his theories of [[special relativity]] and [[general relativity]].  He also made important contributions to [[statistical mechanics]], especially his treatment of [[Brownian motion]], his [[Einstein model|resolution]] of the [[Equipartition theorem#History|paradox]] of [[specific heat capacity|specific heats]], and [[Einstein relation (kinetic theory)|his connection]] of [[fluctuation dissipation theorem|fluctuations and dissipation]].  Despite his reservations about its interpretation, Einstein also made seminal contributions to [[quantum mechanics]] and, indirectly, [[quantum field theory]], primarily through his theoretical studies of the [[photon]].<ref>{{Cite journal | last1 = Whittaker | first1 = E. | authorlink = E. T. Whittaker| doi = 10.1098/rsbm.1955.0005 | title = Albert Einstein. 1879-1955 | journal = [[Biographical Memoirs of Fellows of the Royal Society]] | volume = 1 | pages = 37–67 | date = 1 November 1955| jstor = 769242| title-link = Albert Einstein }}</ref>

The Schilpp numbers are used for cross-referencing in the Notes (the final column of each table), since they cover a greater time period of Einstein's life at present. In addition to his scientific publications, the Schilpp bibliography notes over 130 of Einstein's non-scientific works, often on humanitarian or political topics (pp.&nbsp;730–746). There were also five volumes of Einstein's ''Collected Papers'' (volumes 1, 5, 8–10) that are devoted to his correspondence, much of which is concerned with scientific questions, but were never prepared for publication.

==Chronology and major themes==
[[File:Einstein 1921 by F Schmutzer - restoration.jpg|thumb|upright|alt=A black-and-white photograph of a man standing in front of a chaulk board, holding a piece of chaulk in his right hand, and resting his left hand on a table|[[Albert Einstein|Einstein]] in 1921.]]

The following chronology of Einstein's scientific discoveries provides a context for the publications listed below, and clarifies the major themes running through his work. The first four entries come from his [[Annus Mirabilis papers]] or miracle year papers.

* In 1905, Einstein proposed the existence of the [[photon]], an [[elementary particle]] associated with [[electromagnetic radiation]] (light), which was the foundation of [[Quantum mechanics|quantum theory]].<ref>Pais, pp. 364–388, 402–422.</ref>  In 1909, Einstein showed that the photon carries [[momentum]] as well as energy and that electromagnetic radiation must have both particle-like and wave-like properties if [[Planck's law]] holds; this was a forerunner of the principle of [[wave–particle duality]].<ref>Pais, pp. 402–415.</ref> He would go on to receive the [[List of Nobel laureates in Physics|1921 Nobel Prize in Physics]] for this work.
* In 1905, Einstein developed a theory of [[Brownian motion]] in terms of fluctuations in the number of molecular collisions with an object,<ref>Pais, pp. 93–100.</ref> providing further evidence that matter was composed of [[atom]]s.  A few weeks earlier, he had derived the [[Einstein relation (kinetic theory)|Einstein relation]] for [[diffusion]], which was the first example of the general [[fluctuation-dissipation theorem]] and allowed a good estimate of the [[Avogadro constant]].<ref>Pais, pp. 90–92.</ref>
* In 1905, Einstein developed the theory of [[special relativity]], which reconciled the [[Galilean invariance|relativity of motion]] with the observed constancy of the [[speed of light]] (a paradox of 19th-century physics).<ref>Pais, pp. 111–174.</ref>  Special relativity is now a core principle of physics.  Its counterintuitive predictions that [[time dilation|moving clocks run more slowly]], that [[length contraction|moving objects are shortened in their direction of motion]], and that the [[relativity of simultaneity|order of events is not absolute]] have been confirmed experimentally.
* In 1905, Einstein developed his concept of [[Mass–energy equivalence]]. Its relation ''[[mass–energy equivalence|E=mc<sup>2</sup>]]'' suggested that [[matter]] was a form of energy, which was later verified by the [[mass defect]] in [[atomic nucleus|atomic nuclei]].  The [[Nuclear binding energy|energy]] released in [[nuclear reaction]]s—which is essential for [[nuclear power]] and [[nuclear weapon]]s—can be estimated from such mass defects.<ref name="mass defect">{{cite book | author=Pais A | year = 1988 | title = Inward Bound: Of Matter and Forces in the Physical World | publisher=Oxford University Press | location = Oxford | isbn = 978-0-19-851997-3 | pages = 232–234 | authorlink = Abraham Pais}}</ref>
* In 1907 and again in 1911, Einstein developed [[Einstein solid|the first quantum theory]] of [[specific heat]]s by generalizing Planck's law.<ref>Pais, pp. 389–401.</ref>  His theory resolved a [[Equipartition theorem#History|paradox of 19th-century physics]] that specific heats were often smaller than could be explained by any [[classical physics|classical theory]].  His work was also the first to show that [[Max Planck|Planck's]] [[quantum mechanics|quantum mechanical law]] ''E=hν'' was a fundamental law of physics, and not merely special to [[blackbody radiation]].<ref>Pais, p. 394.</ref>
* Between 1907 and 1915, Einstein developed the theory of [[general relativity]], a classical field theory of [[gravitation]] that provides the cornerstone for modern [[astrophysics]] and [[cosmology]].<ref>Pais, pp. 177–324.</ref>  General relativity is based on the surprising idea that time and [[space]] dynamically interact with [[matter]] and energy, and has been [[tests of general relativity|checked experimentally]] in many ways,<ref>{{cite book | last = Weinberg | first = S | authorlink = Steven Weinberg | year = 1972 | title = Gravitation and Cosmology | publisher = John Wiley and Sons | location = New York | isbn = 978-0-471-92567-5 | pages = [https://archive.org/details/gravitationcosmo00stev_0/page/175 175–210] | url-access = registration | url = https://archive.org/details/gravitationcosmo00stev_0/page/175 }}</ref> confirming its predictions of [[gravitational time dilation|matter affecting the flow of time]],<ref>R.V. Pound and G.A. Rebka, Jr. "Gravitational Red-Shift in Nuclear Resonance" Phys. Rev. Lett. '''3''' 439–441 (1959)
</ref> [[frame dragging]],<ref>Muhlfelder, B., Mac Keiser, G.,  and Turneaure, J., Gravity Probe B Experiment Error, ''poster L1.00027 presented at the American Physical Society (APS) meeting in Jacksonville, Florida, on April 14–17, 2007'', 2007.</ref> [[black hole]]s,<ref>{{cite journal |last1=Gerssen|first1=Joris|last2=van der Marel|first2=Roeland P.| last3=Gebhardt|first3=Karl|last4=Guhathakurta|first4=Puragra|last5=Peterson|first5=Ruth C.|last6=Pryor|first6=Carlton |date=December 2002 |title=Hubble Space Telescope Evidence for an Intermediate-Mass Black Hole in the Globular Cluster M15. II. Kinematic Analysis and Dynamical Modeling |journal=The Astronomical Journal |volume=124 |issue=6 |pages= 3270–3288 |arxiv=astro-ph/0209315 | doi = 10.1086/344584 |bibcode=2002AJ....124.3270G }}<br />{{cite web |url=http://hubblesite.org/newscenter/archive/releases/cosmology/2002/18/text/ |title=Hubble Discovers Black Holes in Unexpected Places |work=HubbleSite |accessdate=2007-10-31 |date=September 17, 2002}}</ref> and [[gravitational wave]]s.<ref>J. M. Weisberg and J. H. Taylor, ''[https://arxiv.org/abs/astro-ph/0407149 Relativistic Binary Pulsar B1913+16: Thirty Years of Observations and Analysis]'', July 2004.</ref>

* In 1917, Einstein published the idea for the [[Einstein–Brillouin–Keller method]] for finding the [[quantum mechanics|quantum mechanical]] version of a classical system.<ref>Pais, p. 412.</ref>  The famous [[Bohr model]] of the hydrogen atom is a simple example, but the EBK method also gives accurate predictions for more complicated systems, such as the dinuclear [[cation]]s H<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup> and HeH<sup>2+</sup>.<ref>{{cite journal | author=Knudson SK | year = 2006 | title = The Old Quantum Theory for H<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup>: Some Chemical Implications | journal=Journal of Chemical Education | volume = 83 | pages = 464–472 | doi = 10.1021/ed083p464 |bibcode = 2006JChEd..83..464K | issue = 3 }}<br />{{cite journal | author=Strand MP, Reinhardt WP | year = 1979 | title = Semiclassical quantization of the low lying electronic states of H<sub>2</sub><sup>+</sup> | journal=Journal of Chemical Physics | volume = 70 | pages = 3812–3827 | doi = 10.1063/1.437932 |bibcode = 1979JChPh..70.3812S | issue = 8 }}</ref>
* In 1918, Einstein developed a general theory of the process by which atoms emit and absorb electromagnetic radiation (his [[Einstein coefficients|''A'' and ''B'' coefficients]]), which is the basis of [[laser]]s ([[stimulated emission]]) and shaped the development of modern [[quantum electrodynamics]], the best-validated physical theory at present.<ref>Pais, pp. 405–407.</ref>
* In 1924, together with [[Satyendra Nath Bose]], Einstein developed the theory of [[Bose–Einstein statistics]] and [[Bose–Einstein condensate]]s, which form the basis for [[superfluidity]], [[superconductivity]], and other phenomena.<ref>Pais, pp. 423–439.</ref>
* In 1935, together with [[Boris Podolsky]] and [[Nathan Rosen]], Einstein put forward what is now known as the [[EPR paradox]], and argued that the quantum-mechanical wave function must be an incomplete description of the physical world.<ref>Pais, pp. 440–459.</ref>
* In the final thirty years of his life, Einstein explored whether various [[classical unified field theories]] could account for both [[electromagnetism]] and gravitation and, possibly, quantum mechanics.  However, his efforts were unsuccessful, since those theories did not match experimental observations.<ref>Pais, pp. 325–354.</ref>
