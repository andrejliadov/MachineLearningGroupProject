This is a list of observed/candidate [[gravitational wave]] events. Direct observation of gravitational waves,{{refn|group=n|Indirect evidence for gravitational waves was obtained by 1978 from observations of orbital decay in the [[Binary pulsar|neutron star binary]] [[PSR B1913+16]].<ref name=npp1993>{{cite web |title=The Nobel Prize in Physics 1993 |publisher=[[Nobel Foundation]] |url=https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/physics/1993/press-release/ |quote=for the discovery of a new type of pulsar, a discovery that has opened up new possibilities for the study of gravitation |accessdate=2018-10-27}}</ref>}} which commenced with the detection of an event by [[LIGO]] in 2015, constitutes part of [[gravitational wave astronomy]]. LIGO has played a role in all subsequent detections to date, with [[Virgo interferometer|Virgo]] joining in August 2017.

==Nomenclature==
Gravitational wave events are named starting with the prefix GW, while observations that trigger an event alert but have not (yet) been confirmed are named starting with the prefix S.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://gcn.gsfc.nasa.gov/lvc.html|title=GCN/LVC Notices|website=Goddard Space Flight Center|access-date=2019-11-11}}</ref> The next two digits indicate the year the event was observed, the middle two digits are the month of observation and the final two digits are the day of the month on which the event was observed. This is similar to the systematic naming for other kinds of astronomical event observations, such as those of [[gamma-ray burst]]s. Probable detections that are not confidently identified as gravitational wave events are designated LVT ("LIGO-Virgo trigger"). Known gravitational wave events come from the merger of two [[black holes]] (BH), two [[neutron stars]] (NS), or a black hole and a neutron star.<ref name="ARX-20181126">{{cite journal |author=Fragione, Giacomo |display-authors=et al. |title=Black Hole and Neutron Star Mergers in Galactic Nuclei |journal=Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society |volume=488 |issue=1 |pages=47–63 |arxiv=1811.10627 |doi=10.1093/mnras/stz1651 |bibcode=2019MNRAS.488...47F |year=2019 |s2cid=85459844 }}</ref><ref name="CNN-20190503">{{cite news |last=Strickland |first=Ashley |title=Scientists may have detected violent collision between neutron star, black hole |url=https://www.cnn.com/2019/05/02/world/gravitational-wave-neutron-star-black-hole-collisions-scn/index.html |date=3 May 2019 |work=[[CNN]] |accessdate=3 May 2019 }}</ref> Some objects are in the [[Stellar black hole#Mass gaps|mass gap]] between the largest predicted neutron star masses ([[Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit]]) and the smallest known black holes.

Observations are made in "runs", three of them so far, with maintenance and upgrades of the detectors made between runs. The first run, O1, ran from 12 September 2015 to 19 January 2016, with O2 from 30 November 2016 to 25 August 2017.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=The LIGO Scientific Collaboration|last2=the Virgo Collaboration|last3=Abbott|first3=B. === Observation candidates from O3/2019 ===
From&nbsp;observation run O3/2019 on, observations are published as [[Open Public Alert]]s to facilitate [[Multi-messenger astronomy|multi-messenger observations]] of events.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://gcn.gsfc.nasa.gov/gcn3_archive.html |title=Real-time alerts and circulars tracker |via=nasa.gov}}</ref><ref name= "LGS2018.10">{{cite web|url= https://www.ligo.org/scientists/GWEMalerts.php |title= Observing Plans and Public Alerts |date= October 2018 | website= www.ligo.org |publisher= LIGO Scientific Collaboration |access-date= 2018-10-28}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=https://wiki.gw-astronomy.org/pub/OpenLVEM/TownHallMeetings2018/what-is-an-opa.pdf |title=What constitutes an open, public alert? |last=Singer |first=Leo P. |date=16 March 2017 |publisher=LSC ([[LIGO Scientific Collaboration]]) |access-date=30 October 2018 |via=gw-astronomy.org}}</ref> Candidate event records can be directly accessed at the Gravitational Wave Candidate Event Database.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://gracedb.ligo.org/latest/ |title=GraceDB — Gravitational Wave Candidate Event Database |via=ligo.org}}</ref> On 1 April 2019, the start of the third observation run was announced with a circular published in the public alerts tracker.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://gcn.gsfc.nasa.gov/gcn3/24045.gcn3 |title=Real-time alerts and circulars tracker |via=nasa.gov}}</ref> The first O3/2019 binary black hole detection alert was broadcast on 8 April 2019. A significant percentage of O3 candidate events detected by LIGO are accompanied by corresponding triggers at Virgo. False alarm rates are mixed, with more than half of events assigned false alarm rates greater than 1 per 20 years, contingent on presence of glitches around signal, foreground electromagnetic instability, seismic activity, and operational status of any one of the three LIGO-Virgo instruments. For instance, events S190421ar and S190425z weren't detected by Virgo and LIGO's Hanford site, respectively.

The LIGO/Virgo collaboration took a short break from observing during the month of October 2019 to improve performance and prepare for future plans, with no signals detected in that month as a result.<ref>{{cite web |title=LIGO Announces Commissioning Break |url=https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/news/ligo20190712 |website=LIGO Lab {{!}} Caltech |accessdate=7 November 2019}}</ref>

The Kamioka Gravitational Wave Detector ([[KAGRA]]) in Japan became operational on 25 February 2020,<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://gwcenter.icrr.u-tokyo.ac.jp/en/archives/1381|title=KAGRA Gravitational-wave Telescope Starts Observation « KAGRA Large-scale Cryogenic Graviationai wave Telescope Project|language=ja|access-date=2020-02-27}}</ref> likely improving the detection and localization of future gravitational wave signals.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/news/ligo20191004|title=KAGRA to Join LIGO and Virgo in Hunt for Gravitational Waves|website=LIGO Lab {{!}} Caltech|access-date=2019-10-06}}</ref> However, KAGRA does not report their signals in real-time on GraceDB as LIGO and Virgo do, so the results of their observation run will likely not be published until the end of O3.

The LIGO-Virgo collaboration ended the O3 run early on March 27, 2020 due to health concerns from the [[COVID-19 pandemic]].<ref name=":0">{{cite web |title=LIGO Suspends Third Observing Run (O3) |url=https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/news/ligo20200326 |website=LIGO Lab |publisher=Caltech |accessdate=20 April 2020}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |title=LIGO Laboratory's Response to COVID-19 |url=https://www.ligo.caltech.edu/news/ligo20200317 |website=LIGO Lab |publisher=Caltech |accessdate=24 March 2020}}</ref>
