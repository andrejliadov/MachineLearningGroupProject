'''Mary Gertrude Sturge Gretton''' born '''May Gertrude Sturge''' later '''Mary Gertrude Henderson''' (1 May 1871 – 5 March 1970) was a [[United Kingdom|British]] historian and magistrate.

==Life==
Gretton was born in [[Gloucester]] on 1 May 1871 when her first name was May. Her father, Joseph Marshall Sturge JP was a merchant, her mother was Anne (Annie) Burke, was a historian. Her father came from a leading abolitionist family and her mother's family had been slave owners.<ref name=maymary>{{Cite web|title=Gretton [née Sturge; first married name Henderson], Mary Gertrude Sturge (1871–1961), historian and magistrate|url=https://www.oxforddnb.com/view/10.1093/ref:odnb/9780198614128.001.0001/odnb-9780198614128-e-59036|access-date=2020-09-13|website=Oxford Dictionary of National Biography|year=2020|language=en|doi=10.1093/odnb/9780198614128.013.59036|last1=Reid|first1=Ellie|isbn=9780198614128}}</ref> She and her younger sister [[Edna Annie Crichton]] attended [[Sidcot School]]. Edna would be Lord mayor of York. Mary went on to another Quaker school [[The Mount School, York|The Mount School]] in York, before going on to study at [[Mason College, Birmingham|Mason College]] in Birmingham.<ref name=maymary/>

In 1898 [[George Cockburn Henderson]] accepted a position at [[Sydney University]] as Acting Professor of History, while the usual professor was on holiday in England. On 5 January 1899, two days before leaving, he and May got married,<ref name=Hamiltonian>{{cite news |url=http://nla.gov.au/nla.news-article132785681 |title=A Distinguished Hamiltonian |newspaper=[[Newcastle Morning Herald and Miners' Advocate]] |issue=7613 |location=New South Wales, Australia |date=5 April 1899 |accessdate=12 September 2020 |page=6 |via=National Library of Australia}}</ref> at the Friends' Meeting Place, [[Leicester]], England. They arrived in Sydney aboard the P & O steamer ''Oceana'' in late February.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://nla.gov.au/nla.news-article113701835 |title=An Australian Scholar Returned |newspaper=[[The Evening News (Sydney)]] |issue=9901 |location=New South Wales, Australia |date=1 March 1899 |accessdate=2 July 2018 |page=4 |via=National Library of Australia}}</ref>

She returned with her husband in 1900 but when he accepted another job in Australia she refused to go or join him later. In 1902 she published ''Three Centuries in North Oxfordshire''<ref>{{Cite book|last=Gretton|first=Mary Sturge|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=BesQAAAAYAAJ&dq=Three+Centuries+in+North+Oxfordshire|title=Three Centuries in North Oxfordshire|date=1902|publisher=B. H. Blackwell|language=en}}</ref> which mixed established history with oral interviews which revealed the opinions of interviewees and their parents about past time.<ref name=maymary/>

In 1910 she was living with Richard Henry Gretton  when was the London editor of the [[Manchester Guardian]] and her estranged husband started divorce proceedings. The divorce was final in 1911 and in 1912 she married Gretton.<ref name=maymary/> In 1912 she abandoned the name of May and adopted the name of Mary. In 1914 she published ''A Corner of the Cotswolds: through the Nineteenth Century''.<ref>{{Cite book|last=Gretton|first=Mary Sturge|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=s1KoCwAAQBAJ&dq=Three+Centuries+in+North+Oxfordshire|title=A Corner of the Cotswolds: through the Nineteenth Century|date=2016-03-01|publisher=BoD – Books on Demand|isbn=978-3-95656-304-1|language=en}}</ref>

In 1921 she was an early woman [[Justice of the Peace]]. She took a strong interest in the probation service and in the Prisoners Aid Society. The Society of Oxford Home Students was allowed to give women degrees and Gretton joined in 1924. She spent some time studying further Oxfordshire history but then undertook a study of [[George Meredith]]. Her ''The Writings and Life of George Meredith'' was not too academic, but she gained a degree in 1926.<ref name=maymary/>

Gretton died in [[York]] in 1970.<ref name=maymary/>
