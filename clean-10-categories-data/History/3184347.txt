'''''Alltagsgeschichte''''' ([[German language|German]]; {{lit|history of the everyday}}) is a form of [[microhistory]] that was particularly prevalent among [[West Germany|West German]] historians during the 1980s. It was founded by [[Alf Luedtke]] and [[:de:Hans Medick|Hans Medick]].<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Lüdtke|first=Alf|date=2003|title=Alltagsgeschichte – ein Bericht von unterwegs|url=https://www.degruyter.com/view/j/ha.2003.11.issue-2/ha.2003.11.2.278/ha.2003.11.2.278.xml|journal=Historische Anthropologie|volume=11|issue=2|pages=278–295|doi=10.7788/ha.2003.11.2.278|issn=2194-4032}}</ref> ''Alltagsgeschichte'' can be considered part of the wider [[Marxianism|Marxian]] historical school of '[[People's history|history from below]]'. It challenged the well-known framework of ''[[:de:Strukturgeschichte|Strukturgeschichte]]'', “structured history”, within the German historical field and advocated for a new model of social history.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Crew|first=David F.|date=1989|title=Alltagsgeschichte: A New Social History "From Below"?|journal=Central European History|language=en|volume=22|issue=3–4|pages=394–407|doi=10.1017/S0008938900020550|issn=1569-1616}}</ref>

== Background ==
''Alltagsgeschichte'' developed from the social and political upheavals of the 1960s when new social movements began to mobilize with political and academic voices.<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal|last=Steege|first=Paul|last2=Bergerson|first2=Andrew Stuart|last3=Healy|first3=Maureen|last4=Swett|first4=Pamela E.|date=2008|title=The History of Everyday Life: A Second Chapter|journal=The Journal of Modern History|language=en|volume=80|issue=2|pages=358–378|doi=10.1086/588855|issn=0022-2801}}</ref> The purpose of ''Alltagsgeschichte'' is to find and prove the links between the everyday, basic experiences of ordinary people in a [[society]], and the broad social and political changes which occur in that society. ''Alltagsgeschichte'' becomes a form of [[microhistory]] because this massively broad endeavour to undertakecan only feasibly be practiced on the most minute of scales. With the political shift in Germany during the 1990s, many historians deemed ''Alltagsgeschichte'' a casualty of the move from social history towards cultural history.<ref name=":0" />

''Alltagsgeschichte''<nowiki/>'s leading proponents include [[Paul Veyne]] and Michel Rouche in France, and Peter Carr in the United Kingdom.<ref>{{Cite book|title=A History of Private Life, Volume I: From Pagan Rome to Byzantium|date=1987–1991|publisher=Belknap Press of Harvard University Press|others=Ariès, Philippe., Duby, Georges., Mazal Holocaust Collection.|isbn=0674399757|location=Cambridge, Mass.|pages=|oclc=13861052}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|title=Portavo : an Irish townland and its peoples|author=Carr, Peter|date=2003–2005|publisher=White Row Press|isbn=1870132114|location=Belfast|oclc=55674102}}</ref> ''Alltagsgeschichte'' can also be linked to the Italian historical doctrine of ''Microstoria'' (microhistory).

== Popular culture ==
An example of ''Alltagsgeschichte'' bleeding into popular culture in Europe can be seen in the Austrian documentary series of the same name, produced between 1985 and 2006 by Elisabeth T. Spira.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=https://der.orf.at/unternehmen/programmangebote/fernsehen/sendungen/sendungen-a-c/alltagsgeschichte100.html|title=Alltagsgeschichte|website=der.ORF.at|language=de|access-date=2019-04-07}}</ref> The series chronicled the everyday lives and stories of Austrian people in over 60 episodes.<ref name=":1" />
