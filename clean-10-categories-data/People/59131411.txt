[[File:The True Life of Billy the Kid (1881) cover (1945 reprint).jpg|thumb|''The True Life of Billy the Kid'' first appeared in print in August 1881]]
Hundreds of songs, books, motion pictures, radio and television programs, and plays have been inspired by the story of the outlaw [[Billy the Kid]].<ref name="Wishart2004">{{cite book|editor=David J. Wishart|author=Jon Tuska|title=Encyclopedia of the Great Plains|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=rtRFyFO4hpEC&pg=PA446|year=2004|publisher=U of Nebraska Press|isbn=0-8032-4787-7|page=446}}</ref> Depictions of him in popular culture have fluctuated between a cold-blooded murderer without a heart and a sentimental hero fighting for justice.<ref name="Reid2004">{{cite book|author=Margaret K. Reid|title=Cultural Secrets as Narrative Form: Storytelling in Nineteenth-century America|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=4FCmNOeyyJoC&pg=PA151|year=2004|publisher=Ohio State University Press|isbn=978-0-8142-0947-9|page=151}}</ref> The Texas historian, [[J. Frank Dobie]], wrote many years ago in ''A Vaquero of the Brush Country'' (1929): "...Billy the Kid will always be interesting, will always appeal to the popular imagination".<ref name="DobieYoung1998">{{cite book|author1=J. Frank Dobie|author2=John D. Young|title=A Vaquero of the Brush Country: The Life and Times of John D. Young|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=NA3RtIWYWhEC&pg=PA169|date=1 August 1998|publisher=University of Texas Press|isbn=978-0-292-78704-9|page=169}}</ref> While a plethora of writers and filmmakers have depicted Billy the Kid as the personification of either heroic youth or juvenile punk,<ref name="Natoli2007">{{cite book|author=Joseph Natoli|title=This Is a Picture and Not the World: Movies and a Post-9/11 America|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=CW8zZ5mzh0wC&pg=PA233|date=8 February 2007|publisher=State University of New York Press|isbn=978-0-7914-7028-2|page=233}}</ref> a few have attempted to portray a more complex character.<ref name="Nolan2014">{{cite book|author=Frederick Nolan|title=The Billy the Kid Reader|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=MwjjBAAAQBAJ&pg=PA363|date=20 October 2014|publisher=University of Oklahoma Press|isbn=978-0-8061-8254-4|page=363}}</ref> In any case, the dramatic aspects of his short life and violent death still appeal to popular taste,<ref name="Tatum1982190">{{cite book|author=Stephen Tatum|title=Inventing Billy the Kid: Visions of the Outlaw in America, 1881-1981|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=P4jWAAAAMAAJ|date=1 January 1982|publisher=University of New Mexico Press|isbn=978-0-8263-0610-4|page=190}}</ref> and he remains an icon of [[teenage rebellion]] and [[Counterculture|nonconformity]].<ref name="Wallis2007">{{cite book|author=Michael Wallis|title=Billy the Kid: The Endless Ride|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=qViLb3BCaAAC&pg=xvi|year=2007|publisher=W.W. Norton & Company|isbn=978-0-393-06068-3|page=xvi}}</ref> The mythologizing of his story continues with new works in various media.<ref name="FeeWebb2016">{{cite book|editor=[[Christopher R. Fee]], Jeffrey B. Webb|title=American Myths, Legends, and Tall Tales: An Encyclopedia of American Folklore (3 Volumes)|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=kXnEDAAAQBAJ&pg=PA145|date=31 August 2016|publisher=ABC-CLIO|isbn=978-1-61069-568-8|pages=145–146}}</ref><ref name="Slatta2001">{{cite book|author=Richard W. Slatta|title=The Mythical West: An Encyclopedia of Legend, Lore, and Popular Culture|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=iczSBcAUC5oC&pg=PA47|year=2001|publisher=ABC-CLIO|isbn=978-1-57607-151-9|page=47}}</ref>

== Music ==
* "Billy the Kid" is a [[western music (North America)|western folk song]] in the public domain, which was published in [[John A. Lomax]] and [[Alan Lomax]]'s ''American Ballads and Folksongs'' album,<ref>MacMillan, (1934), p. 137</ref> and also their ''Cowboy Songs and Other Frontier Ballads'' album.<ref>MacMillan, (1938), pp. 140–141. From Jim Marby, recorded in 1911, Library of Congress E659098.</ref> Members of the [[Western Writers of America]] chose it as one of the Top 100 Western songs of all time.<ref name="Top100">{{cite web |title = The Top 100 Western Songs |author = Western Writers of America |year = 2010 |author-link = Western Writers of America |publisher = American Cowboy |url = http://www.americancowboy.com/culture/top-100-western-songs |archive-url = https://www.webcitation.org/6RjFQXqGy?url=http://www.americancowboy.com/culture/top-100-western-songs |archive-date = August 10, 2014 |url-status = dead |df = mdy }}</ref> The song's lyrics perpetuate several myths about the Kid, such as his exaggerated kill count of "twenty-one men" and the suggestion that [[Pat Garrett]] had known him as a friend.
* There have been many recordings of this song, including versions by the [[Sons of the Pioneers]] (1937), [[Woody Guthrie]] (1940, recorded by Alan Lomax for the Library of Congress #3412 B<sub>2</sub>), [[Tex Ritter]] (1945), and [[Marty Robbins]] (1959, on his ''[[Gunfighter Ballads and Trail Songs]]'' album).<ref>{{cite web |url = http://www.last.fm/music/Marty+Robbins/_/Billy+the+Kid |title = Marty Robbins Billy the Kid (2:19) |publisher = Last FM |access-date = February 6, 2016 |url-status = live |archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20150523050246/http://www.last.fm/music/Marty+Robbins/_/Billy+the+Kid |archive-date = May 23, 2015 |df = mdy-all }}</ref>
* Woody Guthrie later used the same melody for his song "So Long, it's Been Good to Know You". He also recorded it in 1944 for Moe Asch's Asch/Folkways label (MA67).<ref>{{cite web |title = Liner notes: Woody Guthrie / Buffalo Skinners: The Asch Recordings Vol 4 / Number 3: Billy the Kid |publisher = Smithsonian Folkways |url = http://media.smithsonianfolkways.org/liner_notes/smithsonian_folkways/SFW40112.pdf |access-date = January 7, 2010 |url-status = live |archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20120301205641/http://media.smithsonianfolkways.org/liner_notes/smithsonian_folkways/SFW40112.pdf |archive-date = March 1, 2012 |df = mdy-all }}</ref>
