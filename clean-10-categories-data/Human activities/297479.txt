'''''As the crow flies''''', similar to ''in a [[wikt:beeline|beeline]]'', is an [[idiom]] for the most direct path between two points. This meaning is attested from the early 19th century,<ref name="Allen"/><ref name="Knowles"/> and appeared in [[Charles Dickens]]'s novel ''[[Oliver Twist]]'':

{{quote|We cut over the fields at the back with him between us – straight as the crow flies – through hedge and ditch.<ref name="Allen"/>}}

Crows do conspicuously fly alone across open country, but neither crows nor bees (as in “beeline”) fly in particularly straight lines.<ref name=BBC>Villazon, Luis.  [http://www.sciencefocus.com/article/do-crows-actually-fly-straight-line “Do crows actually fly in a straight line?”], [[BBC Focus]] (August 30, 2017).</ref> Crows do not swoop in the air like [[swallow]]s or [[starling]]s, and often circle above their nests.<ref name=BBC />  One claim, that before modern navigational methods were introduced, crows were kept upon ships and released when land was sought,<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://see-the-sea.org/nautical/naut-body.htm|title=See the Sea - Nautical Language|website=see-the-sea.org}}</ref> has no scientific basis. In fact crows would not travel well in cages as they fight if confined.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.worldwidewords.org/qa/qa-ast1.htm|title=World Wide Words: As the crow flies|website=World Wide Words}}</ref>
