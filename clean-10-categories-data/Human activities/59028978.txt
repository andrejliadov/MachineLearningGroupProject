A '''rage room''', also known as a '''smash room''' or '''anger room''', is a business where people can vent their [[Rage (emotion)|rage]] by destroying objects within a room.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.nbcnews.com/nightly-news/video/rage-rooms-where-americans-go-to-take-out-their-frustrations-713958979607?v=railb&|title='Rage Rooms': Where Americans Go to Take Out Their Frustrations|publisher=}}</ref><ref name="usatoday.com">{{cite web|url=https://www.usatoday.com/story/news/2018/11/11/rage-rooms-what-might-surprise-you-growing-trend/1754653002/|title=Rage rooms: Why recreational smashing could be good for your mental health|publisher=}}</ref>

Rage rooms may consist of fake living rooms and kitchens with replicas of furnishings and objects such as televisions and desks. The company may allow clients to bring their own possessions to destroy.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://abcnews.go.com/Business/anger-room-texas-stressed-customers-smash-tvs-junk/story?id=16315732|title=Stressed Out? Enter the 'Anger Room'|date=10 May 2012|website=ABC News}}</ref>

The first rage rooms to open were likely in Japan in 2008 or earlier.<ref name="vice">{{cite web|url=https://www.vice.com/en_asia/article/4xyqbm/a-look-inside-rage-rooms-where-you-de-stress-by-smashing-things-en-id|title=A Look Inside &#39;Rage Rooms,&#39; Where You De-Stress by Smashing Things|website=VICE|accessdate=2018-11-12}}</ref><ref name="telegraph">{{cite web|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/newstopics/howaboutthat/3909595/Stressed-Japanese-workers-smash-plates-to-ease-recession-blues.html|title=Stressed Japanese workers smash plates to ease recession blues|website=Telegraph|accessdate=2018-11-12}}</ref><ref name="forbes">{{cite web|url=https://www.forbes.com/sites/lesliewu/2016/10/30/all-the-rage-scream-and-anger-rooms-are-boiling-up-around-the-world/#1b8ea1731ef1|website=forbes.com|title=All The Rage: Scream and Anger Rooms Are Boiling Up Around The World|accessdate=2018-11-12}}</ref> The concept spread to other countries, such as Serbia, England, and Argentina. Today, there are hundreds of rage rooms in cities in the United States such as [[Denver, Colorado|Denver]], [[Huntsville, Alabama|Huntsville]], [[Tucson, Arizona|Tucson]], [[Austin, Texas|Austin]], [[Milwaukee]], [[Buffalo, New York|Buffalo]], [[Charleston, South Carolina|Charleston]], [[Rochester, New York|Rochester]], [[Hampton, Virginia|Hampton]], [[Eugene, Oregon|Eugene]], [[Charlotte, North Carolina|Charlotte]], [[Ogden, Utah|Ogden]] and [[American Fork, Utah|American Fork]].<ref name="usatoday.com"/>

==Effectiveness==
In January 2017, a study led by [[Michael Stevens (educator)|Michael Stevens]] on rage rooms showed that they are not effective in controlling anger, and in some cases, may actually make participants more angry.<ref>{{cite episode|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zD68reVP0Ek|date=January 25, 2017|title=Destruction|series=Mind Field|season=1|number=3|first=Michael|last=Stevens}}</ref>

In an article published in July 2019, Dr. [[John Huber (psychologist)|John Huber]], a clinical forensic psychologist, on rage rooms stated "Therapy is very beneficial all the way around. I think this is a stopgap. I think if you use it in conjunction with therapy this would be great."<ref>{{cite web|url=https://spectrumlocalnews.com/tx/austin/news/2019/07/04/new-kyle-business-offers-rage-relief--|date=July 4, 2019|title=New Kyle Business offers Rage Relief}}</ref>
