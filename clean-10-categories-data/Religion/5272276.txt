'''Syed Usman Marwandi'''<ref name="ansari">Sarah Ansari (1992) ''Sufi Saints and State Power: The Pirs of Sindh, 1843–1947''. Vanguard Books</ref> (1177 &ndash; 1274), popularly known as '''Lal Shahbaz Qalandar''' ({{lang-sd|لعل شھباز قلندر}}), was a [[Sufism|Sufi]] saint and religious-poet of present-day [[Pakistan]] and [[Afghanistan]].<ref name=PakistanToday/> He is highly regarded and respected by people of all religions because he preached religious tolerance among Muslims and Hindus.<ref name=PakistanToday/><ref name=Dawn/> He was called ''Lal'' ("ruby-colored") after his usual red attire and "[[Shahbaz (bird)|Shahbaz]]" to denote a noble and divine spirit and "[[Qalandar (title)|Qalandar]]" as he was a wandering holy man.<ref name=PakistanToday/> The spiritual song "[[Dama Dam Mast Qalandar]]" glorifies Lal Shahbaz Qalandar's teachings, and the song is widely used in Pakistan, India, Afghanistan and Bangladesh. Originally written in the 13th century, the song has been sung by various singers since then, and is widely popular in the sub-continent.

== Life ==
Lal Shahbaz Qalandar, son of Ibrahim Kabeeruddin,<ref name=Dawn>{{Cite news|url=http://www.dawn.com/news/1066533|title=Saint revered by people of all religions|author=Masood Lohar|date=5 October 2004|publisher=DAWN (newspaper)|access-date=14 February 2018|language=en}}</ref> was born in [[Maiwand]], today's [[Afghanistan]].<ref name=PakistanToday>{{Cite web|url=https://www.pakistantoday.com.pk/2012/07/09/the-sufi-in-red/|title=The Sufi in red|date=9 July 2012|publisher=Pakistan Today (newspaper)|access-date=14 February 2018}}</ref> His ancestors had migrated from [[Baghdad]], Iraq and settled in [[Mashhad]], Iran before moving to [[Maiwand]]. He lived when the [[Ghaznavids|Ghaznavid]] and [[Ghurids]] ruled in the Indus region (today's [[Punjab]], [[Pakistan]]).<ref>N B G Qazi (1971) ''Lal Shahbaz Qalandar Uthman Marwandi''. [[RCD Cultural Institute]].</ref> Lal Shahbaz Qalandar was a [[Sufi]] Muslim.<ref>{{citation|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=f2KPGwAACAAJ|title=An Illustrated History of Sindh|publisher=Heritage Foundation|year=2002|page=78}}</ref>

A contemporary of [[Jalal ad-Din Muhammad Balkhi-Rumi|Rumi]], he travelled around the [[Muslim world]] and settled in [[Sehwan]], [[Sindh]] where he was eventually buried.<ref name="inam">M Inam (1978) ''Hazrat Lal Shahbaz Qalandar of Sehwan Sharif''. Karachi.</ref> There is evidence of his presence in Sindh in 1196 when he met Pir Haji Ismail Panhwar of [[Paat]] and he is believed to have arrived in [[Sehwan]] around 1251. There he established a meeting house (''[[khanqah]]''), taught in the Fuqhai Islam Madarrsah and wrote his treatises ''Mizan-us-Surf'', ''Kism-e-Doyum'', ''Aqd'' and ''Zubdah''. Lal Shahbaz lived a celibate life.<ref name=PakistanToday/>

In [[Multan]], he met [[Baha-ud-din Zakariya]] of the [[Suhrawardiyya]] order, Baba [[Fariduddin Ganjshakar]] of the [[Chishtiyya]] and [[Syed Jalaluddin Bukhari]]. The friendship of these four became legendary, they were known as the Chahar Yar (In [[Persian language|Persian]] "the four friends").<ref name=Dawn/> According to some historians, the four friends visited various parts of Sindh, Punjab (in present-day Pakistan) and southern part of India.<ref name=Dawn/>

Shahbaz became a profound scholar of religions, fluent in many languages including [[Pashto language|Pashto]], [[Persian language|Persian]], [[Turkish language|Turkish]], [[Arabic language|Arabic]], [[Sindhi language|Sindhi]] and [[Sanskrit]]. This was also the time period when [[Ghiyas ud din Balban]] (reigned: 1266 &ndash; 1287) ruled [[India]].<ref name=Dawn/>

Following his death, Hindus within Sindh began to identify Lal Shahbaz Qalandar as an incarnation of their [[Iṣṭa-devatā|patron deity]], [[Jhulelal (Hinduism)|Jhulelal]]. This connection was emphasized by the popular spiritual song ''[[Dama Dam Mast Qalandar]]'' which referred to him by the name Jhulelal. Over time, the Jhulelal has become a nickname for him, among both Hindu and Muslim Sindhis.<ref name=PakistanToday/>

== Shrine ==
{{Main|Shrine of Lal Shahbaz Qalandar}}
[[File:Lal Shahbaz Mazaar inside view 6.JPG|thumb|266px|Interior of the shrine of Lal Shahbaz Qalandar in [[Sehwan]]]]
The shrine of Lal Shahbaz Qalandar was built by [[Firuz Shah Tughlaq|Feroz Shah Tughlaq]] in 1356,<ref>{{Cite book|last=Hasan|first=Masudul|title=Hand Book of Important Places in West Pakistan|publisher=Pakistan Social Service Foundation|year=1965|location=Lahore|pages=21}}</ref> expanded by Mirza Jani Beg<ref>{{Cite book|last=Balfour|first=Edward|title=The Cyclopædia of India and of Eastern and Southern Asia, Volume 3|publisher=B. Quaritch|year=1885|pages=562}}</ref> and his son Mirza Ghazi Beg of [[Tarkhan dynasty|Tarkhan Dynasty]], but was not completed until 1639, when Nawab Dindar Khan paved the courtyard with glazed tiles.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|title=Journal of the Asiatic Society of Bengal, Volume 68|publisher=Bishop's College Press|year=1899|location=Asia|pages=32}}</ref> The silver work on the gate, the balustrade around the tomb and the top of the dome was gifted by Mir Karam Ali Talpur of [[Talpur dynasty|Talpur Dynasty]].<ref name=":0" /> Later on the shrine was decorated with Sindhi 'kashi-tiles', mirror-work and a gold-plated door donated by the [[Reza Shah Pahlavi|Shah of Iran, Reza Shah Pahlavi]] and installed by the late Prime Minister of Pakistan, [[Zulfikar Ali Bhutto]].<ref name="mathyani">N M Mathyani (2002) [http://www.jafariyanews.com/oct2k2/26_qalandar.htm Lal Shahbaz Qalandar: A great saint]. jafariyanews.com</ref> The inner sanctum is about 100 square yards with a silver-canopied grave in the middle, according to Nadeem Wagan, Cutharo silver donated by Sardar Mahboob Ali Khan Wagan (Chief Sardar of Wagan Tribe) on one side of the marble floor is a row of about {{convert|12|in|mm|adj=mid|-high}} folding wooden stands, on which there is a set of copies of the [[Quran]] for devotees to read. On the other side, beside a bundle of incense, are rows of oil-lamps lighted by devotees. Thousands of devotees visit the tomb particularly every Thursday.<ref name=reuters/><ref name=TNI/>

=== Mela / Urs (Annual Fair) ===
Lal Shahbaz's annual ''[[Urs]]'' (death anniversary), held on the 18 [[Sha'aban]] – the eighth month of the [[Muslim calendar|Muslim lunar calendar]], brings more than two million pilgrims from all over Pakistan and parts of India, Bangladesh. Essentially it’s a south Asian affair.<ref name=Tribune>{{Cite news|url=https://tribune.com.pk/story/1412392/glory-qalandars-urs-culminates-sehwan/|title=In all its glory, Qalandar's urs culminates in Sehwan|date=18 May 2017|work=The Express Tribune (newspaper)|access-date=14 February 2018|language=en-US}}</ref> On each morning of the three-day feast, the narrow lanes of Sehwan are packed with pilgrims, ''fakirs'' and devotees making their way to the shrine to commune with the saint, offer tributes and ask for their wishes.
(''Sao Sumar'') singing from 6am till 8am the next day.  They invite bands of folk-singers (''mandali'') from different regions each year. There have been many women who have claimed to be sexually assaulted during the overnight festivities and there have also been accusations of police indifference and coverups for such crimes.

=== The 2017 terrorist attack ===
On 16 February 2017, a group claimed responsibility for a [[2017 Sehwan suicide bombing|suicide attack on the shrine]] which resulted in the deaths of 88 people.<ref name=TNI>{{cite news|title=Sehwan bombing toll reaches 88, over 250 injured|url=https://www.thenews.com.pk/latest/186965-Sehwan-bombing-toll-reaches-80-over-250-injured|access-date=14 February 2018|publisher=The News International (newspaper)|date=17 February 2017}}</ref> The following morning, the shrine's caretaker continued the daily tradition of ringing the shrine's bell at 3:30 A.M. and defiantly vowed that he would not be intimidated by the terrorists. Pakistani government and security forces have also launched a nationwide security crackdown and have recently killed 37 terrorists.<ref>{{cite news|title=37 terrorists killed in security crackdown after Sehwan bombing|url=https://www.thenews.com.pk/latest/186962-37-terrorists-killed-in-security-crackdown-after-Sehwan-bombing|access-date=14 February 2018|publisher=The News International (newspaper)|date=17 February 2017}}</ref> The shrine's ''dhamaal'', or meditative dancing ceremony, was resumed the very next evening following the attack.<ref name=reuters>{{cite news|title=Pakistan's Sufis defiant after Islamic State attack on shrine kills 83|url=https://www.reuters.com/article/us-pakistan-blast-sufis-idUSKBN15W1OP|access-date=14 February 2018|agency=Reuters News Agency|date=17 February 2017}}</ref>

== In poetry and prose ==

''"Lal Meri Pat Rakhiyo ustad Fareed Ayaz and Abu Mohammad..."''(See [[Dama Dam Mast Qalandar]]). This poem was initially written by [[Amir Khusrow]], then further modified by [[Bulleh Shah|Baba Bulleh shah]]. Lal Shabaz Qalandar has also been referred to as "[[Jhulelal (Sufism)|Jhulelal]]".
