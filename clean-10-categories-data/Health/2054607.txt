The '''age of onset''' is the [[senescence|age]] at which an individual acquires, develops, or first experiences a condition or symptoms of a [[disease]] or disorder. For instance, the general age of onset for the [[spinal column|spinal]] disease [[scoliosis]] is "10-15 years old,"<ref>{{Cite web | url=http://www.scoliosis.org/info.php | title=National Scoliosis Foundation}}</ref> meaning that most people develop [[scoliosis]] when they are of an age between ten and fifteen years. 

Diseases are often categorized by their ages of onset as [[Congenital disorder|congenital]], [[Infant|infantile]], [[Child|juvenile]], or [[adult]]. Missed or delayed diagnosis often occurs if a disease that is typically diagnosed in juveniles (such as asthma) is present in adults, and vice versa (such as arthritis).<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Kirkpatrick|first1=Susan|last2=Locock|first2=Louise|last3=Farre|first3=Albert|last4=Ryan|first4=Sara|last5=Salisbury|first5=Helen|last6=McDonagh|first6=Janet E.|date=2018-02-09|title=Untimely illness: When diagnosis does not match age-related expectations|journal=Health Expectations|volume=21|issue=4|pages=730–740|doi=10.1111/hex.12669|pmid=29424066|pmc=6117493|issn=1369-6513}}</ref> Depending on the disease, ages of onset may impact features such as phenotype, as is the case in Parkinson's and Huntington's diseases.<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal|last1=Pagano|first1=Gennaro|last2=Ferrara|first2=Nicola|last3=Brooks|first3=David J.|last4=Pavese|first4=Nicola|date=2016-02-10|title=Age at onset and Parkinson disease phenotype|journal=Neurology|volume=86|issue=15|pages=1400–1407|doi=10.1212/wnl.0000000000002461|pmid=26865518|pmc=4831034|issn=0028-3878}}</ref><ref name=":1">{{Cite journal|last1=Sipilä|first1=Jussi O. T.|last2=Kauko|first2=Tommi|last3=Päivärinta|first3=Markku|last4=Majamaa|first4=Kari|date=2017-08-28|title=Comparison of mid-age-onset and late-onset Huntington's disease in Finnish patients|journal=Journal of Neurology|volume=264|issue=10|pages=2095–2100|doi=10.1007/s00415-017-8600-2|pmid=28849405|s2cid=37872535|issn=0340-5354}}</ref> For example, the phenotype for juvenile Huntington's disease clearly differs from adult-onset Huntington's disease and late-onset Parkinson's exhibits more severe motor and non-motor phenotypes.<ref name=":0" /><ref name=":1" />

== Theories of Aging ==
The '''rate-of-living''' theory of aging states that senescence occurs because individuals accumulate damage to cells and tissues during cell division. This theory is not supported because its postulates that aging rate should be correlated with metabolic rate<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Austad|first1=S. N.|last2=Fischer|first2=K. Gerontology|volume=46|issue=2|pages=B47–B53|doi=10.1093/geronj/46.2.b47|pmid=1997563|issn=0022-1422}}</ref> and organisms cannot evolve longer lifespans<ref name=":2">{{Cite journal|last=Rose|first=Michael R.|date=September 1984|title=Laboratory Evolution of Postponed Senescence in Drosophila There are two subsets to the '''evolutionary''' theory of aging: [[antagonistic pleiotropy hypothesis]] and the [[mutation accumulation]] hypothesis.

The [[antagonistic pleiotropy hypothesis]] was tested by monitoring the ''age-1'' gene in [[C. elegans]].<ref name=":4">{{Cite journal|last1=Walker|first1=David W.|last2=McColl|first2=Gawain|last3=Jenkins|first3=Nicole L.|last4=Harris|first4=Jennifer|last5=Lithgow|first5=Gordon J.|date=May 2000|title=Evolution of lifespan in C. elegans|journal=Nature|volume=405|issue=6784|pages=296–297|doi=10.1038/35012693|pmid=10830948|s2cid=4402039|issn=0028-0836}}</ref> The ''age-1'' gene plays a role in senescence; nematodes with mutations in this gene live up to 80% longer.<ref name=":4" /> Mutants in the ''age-1'' gene for allele ''hx546'' seem to be otherwise normal until placed under stressful conditions.<ref name=":4" /> Then, the carriers of the mutant gene appear to be at disadvantage—they do not lay eggs while being starved.<ref name=":4" /> This evidence supports antagonistic pleiotropy as a theory of aging, and therefore as an onset cause in some cases.

The mutation accumulation hypothesis was tested by demonstrating how quickly deleterious mutations can accumulate in [[Musca domestica]].<ref name=":5">{{Cite journal|last1=Reed|first1=David H|last2=Bryant|first2=Edwin H|date=2000|title=The evolution of senescence under curtailed life span in laboratory populations of Musca domestica (the housefly)|journal=Heredity|volume=85|issue=2|pages=115–121|doi=10.1046/j.1365-2540.2000.00737.x|pmid=11012712|issn=0018-067X|doi-access=free}}</ref> Reed and Bryant demonstrated this by limiting the lifespan of the flies to a few days, which made late-life mutations invisible to selection since they occurred after reproduction.<ref name=":5" /> The lifespan of the flies was monitored by allowing them to carry out their complete lifespan every few generations, which was reported to decline substantially.<ref name=":5" /> Mutation accumulation is supported as a theory of aging, and therefore an onset cause in cases of diseases resulting from mutation accumulation.
