Disease emergence and resurgence in populations of wild animals are considered an important topic for conservationists, as these diseases can affect the sustainability of affected populations and the long-term survival of some species.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Smith|first1=K. == Prevention ==

=== Culling ===

Disease outbreaks in wild animals are sometimes controlled by killing infected individuals to prevent transmission to domestic and economically important animals.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Harrison|first1=Annabel|last2=Newey|first2=Scott|last3=Gilbert|first3=Lucy|last4=Haydon|first4=Daniel T.|last5=Thirgood|first5=Simon|date=2010|title=Culling wildlife hosts to control disease: mountain hares, red grouse and louping ill virus|journal=Journal of Applied Ecology|language=en|volume=47|issue=4|pages=926–930|doi=10.1111/j.1365-2664.2010.01834.x|issn=1365-2664}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Cowled|first1=Brendan D.|last2=Garner|first2=M. Graeme|last3=Negus|first3=Katherine|last4=Ward|first4=Michael P.|date=2012-01-16|title=Controlling disease outbreaks in wildlife using limited culling: modelling classical swine fever incursions in wild pigs in Australia|journal=Veterinary Research|volume=43|issue=1|pages=3|doi=10.1186/1297-9716-43-3|issn=1297-9716|pmc=3311561|pmid=22243996}}</ref> [[Animal rights]] advocates argue against culling, as they consider individual wild animals to be intrinsically valuable and believe that they have a right to live.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Killing Wildlife: The Pros and Cons of Culling Animals|url=https://www.nationalgeographic.com/news/2014/3/140305-culling-badgers-deer-bison-swans-ethics-conservation/|last=James|first=Will|date=2014-03-06|website=National Geographic News|language=en|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2020-05-17}}</ref>

=== Vaccination programs ===

[[File:Rabies vaccine in bait.jpg|thumb|Oral rabies vaccine in bait]]

[[Wild animal suffering]], as a result of disease, has been drawn attention to by some authors,<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Tomasik|first=Brian|date=2015|title=The Importance of Wild-Animal Suffering|url=https://heinonline.org/HOL/Page?handle=hein.journals/relations3&id=129&div=&collection=|journal=Relations: Beyond Anthropocentrism|volume=3|issue=2|pages=133–152|doi=10.7358/rela-2015-002-toma|doi-access=free}}</ref> who argue that we should alleviate this form of suffering through [[Animal vaccination|vaccination]] programs.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Wild animals endure illness, injury, and starvation. We should help.|url=https://www.vox.com/2015/12/14/9873012/wild-animals-suffering|last=Anthis|first=Jacy Reese|date=2015-12-14|website=Vox|language=en|access-date=2020-05-17}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Faria|first1=Catia|last2=Paez|first2=Eze|date=2015|title=Animals in Need: The Problem of Wild Animal Suffering and Intervention in Nature|url=https://heinonline.org/HOL/Page?handle=hein.journals/relations3&id=3&div=&collection=|journal=Relations: Beyond Anthropocentrism|volume=3|pages=7}}</ref> Such programs are also deemed beneficial for reducing the exposure of humans and domestic animals to disease and for species conservation.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Wildlife Vaccination - Growing in Feasibility?|url=https://cwhl.vet.cornell.edu/article/wildlife-vaccination-growing-feasibility|last=Abbott|first=Rachel C.|date=2020-02-17|website=Cornell Wildlife Health Lab|language=en|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2020-05-17}}</ref>

The oral [[rabies vaccine]] has been used successfully in multiple countries to control the spread of rabies among populations of wild animals and reduce human exposure.<ref name="A">{{cite web|title=Oral Rabies Vaccination|url=https://www.aphis.usda.gov/aphis/ourfocus/wildlifedamage/programs/nwrc/research-areas/SA_Rabies/CT_Orv_vaccination|last=|first=|date=2019-09-23|website=[[Animal and Plant Health Inspection Service]] (APHIS)|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|accessdate=12 November 2019}}</ref> Australia, the UK, Spain and New Zealand have all conducted successful vaccination programs to prevent [[Bovine Tuberculosis]], by vaccinating badgers, possums and wild boar.<ref>{{Cite web|title=The Case for Wild Animal Vaccination|url=https://www.natureethics.org/words/2018/3/3/the-case-for-wild-animal-vaccination|last=Quellette|first=Cara|date=|website=Nature Ethics|language=en-US|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2020-05-17}}</ref>

In response to the [[COVID-19 pandemic]], it has been proposed that, in the future, wild animals could be vaccinated against [[coronavirus]]es to relieve the suffering of the affected animals, prevent disease transmission and inform future vaccination efforts.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Helping wild animals through vaccination: could this happen for coronaviruses like SARS-CoV-2?|url=https://www.animal-ethics.org/helping-wild-animals-vaccination-coronaviruses-sars-cov-2/|last=|first=|date=2020-05-12|website=Animal Ethics|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2020-05-17}}</ref>
